Category Positioning

The category frame

Sellers in this category would search "sell my car", "cash for cars [suburb]" or "car buyers near me" — the same terms every business in competitor-analysis.html is built to catch. But what they would call the business to a friend splits sharply by which one they used: "I used carsales" or "a cash-for-cars mob came out" versus "Adam Hall came and bought it" — a person's name, not a company. That difference is the category opportunity.

What this business is compared against, per the businesses verified in competitor-analysis.html: CarBuyers and We Buy Cars (branch- or franchise-style direct buyers), carsales Instant Offer (a national platform's instant-offer feature), and, as fallback options rather than active choices, private sale marketplaces and dealership trade-in. [Inferred from D06/DS hypothesis, corroborated by the competitive set above] — Adam has not yet confirmed in his own words which of these he is actually compared against by real customers (discovery-dataset.html Q15, Q24 partial).

The positioning map

The two axes buyers in this category actually decide on, based on the messaging each competitor leads with in competitor-analysis.html: Price-maximising ↔ Trust-and-ease-maximising (what the seller is optimising for), and Self-service/digital-first ↔ Fully human/personal (how the transaction is delivered).

The crowded corner is price-led, branch- or platform-delivered — CarBuyers, We Buy Cars and carsales Instant Offer all compete there, and it is where their reputational damage (post-inspection price drops, "best price" scepticism, documented in competitor-analysis.html) concentrates. The empty space is trust-and-ease-maximising, fully human and personal — no competitor found in this research occupies it credibly. Adam Hall Buy My Car already sits there by construction, not by marketing choice yet.

Positions taken

  • carsales Instant Offer — "the trusted national default", held firmly through sheer platform scale and a data-backed pricing claim (83% above trade-in). Web research.
  • CarBuyers — "the best price guarantee", held less firmly: the claim is public and prominent, but ProductReview evidence (3.2/5, 184 reviews) shows real customers contesting it. Web research.
  • We Buy Cars — "the no-nonsense, no-tyre-kicker cash buyer", held through a dense SE Queensland branch network and blunt tone; local reputation to back the claim is unconfirmed in this research. Web research.
  • Private sale — "the most money, if you'll do the work" — held by default, not by any single business's marketing. Assumption.
  • Dealership trade-in — "the safe, lazy default" — held through bundling with a new-car purchase rather than through any trust claim. Assumption.

The position worth claiming

"The name you already trust, at your kitchen table." Not the cheapest, not the fastest app, not a branch to drive to — the person whose voice sellers already know, coming to them, giving a number that does not move.

Why customers value it. Discovery data (discovery-dataset.html Q2, Q9, Q12) establishes this audience is trust-first and price-second, fears the process itself (paperwork, technology, strangers) more than losing a few hundred dollars, and wants relief rather than a negotiation win. That is a direct match to a position none of the three named direct competitors occupy — all three are companies or platforms, not people, and CarBuyers/We Buy Cars-style reviews show the price-first promise repeatedly breaking down at the point of inspection (competitor-analysis.html).

Why it's winnable. Adam already has the two hardest-to-build ingredients: 27+ years of trade credibility and a decade-long public radio relationship generating 10,000+ on-air valuations (discovery-dataset.html Q4, Q5). No competitor identified in this research has an equivalent public, personal reputation asset.

Why incumbents won't easily follow. CarBuyers and We Buy Cars are built for branch- or panel-based scale; carsales Instant Offer is built to route sellers to a rotating dealer network. All three have business models optimised for volume and geographic reach, not for a single accountable relationship — copying Adam's model would mean giving up the very structure that lets them operate nationally or across multiple branches. This is a structural reason, not just a marketing choice, and it is why the position is defensible rather than merely first-mover.
