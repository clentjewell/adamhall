Why each gap is open

  • Gaps 1, 3, 6 (non-digital sellers, estate/health-crisis, paperwork reassurance) are open because they are largely invisible to the researched competitors' business models: CarBuyers, We Buy Cars and carsales Instant Offer are all built around an online quote or app-first entry point, which structurally filters out the least digitally confident sellers before they ever reach a human. It is not that these competitors have chosen to ignore this need — their funnels are simply not designed to surface it.
  • Gap 2 (a number that doesn't move) is open because it is off-strategy for panel- and branch-based buyers: their business model depends on inspecting the car before committing final price, and reviews cited in competitor-analysis.html show that gap being exploited (intentionally or not) often enough to be a pattern, not an accident.
  • Gap 4 (Northern Rivers coverage) is open because it sits below the density threshold that justifies a branch (We Buy Cars) or a dedicated local SEO push (CarBuyers, carsales) — genuinely uneconomic for them to chase at their cost base, not a deliberate concession.
  • Gap 5 (trust in the promise itself) is open because it is a reputational hole the price-led competitors have partly dug themselves, via the exact review pattern documented in competitor-analysis.html — hard for them to close without changing the underlying model that created it.
  • Gap 7 (systemised referral) is open because none of the researched competitors have a public reason to build one — they acquire customers primarily through paid SEO and platform traffic, not personal reputation, so a referral engine is not a natural extension of their existing strategy the way it is for Adam's radio-and-reputation base.

Qualitative sizing

No customer volume, spend or conversion numbers exist for any of these gaps yet — discovery-dataset.html flags customer lifetime value (Q18), lead-source split (Q14/Q19) and marketing spend (Q31) as [NOT AVAILABLE — ask client]. The following are directional qualifiers only, flagged as hypothesis pending CORE question answers (CQ06–CQ08), not invented numbers.

  • Estate/health-crisis sellers (gap 3): plausibly a meaningful minority of Adam's existing ~25 cars/month, given testimonials already skew toward this scenario (discovery-dataset.html Q20) — likely a segment worth naming explicitly rather than a large untapped pool. Willingness to pay is likely no higher than the general base; the value is in trust and speed, not price sensitivity.
  • Northern Rivers coverage (gap 4): smaller population base than Gold Coast/Brisbane, so likely a lower-volume but low-competition pocket — worth holding, not necessarily worth large paid-media spend against, until lead volume from the area is confirmed.
  • The "number that doesn't move" promise (gap 2): hard to size directly, but the volume and intensity of negative reviews citing this exact complaint across CarBuyers and similar operators (competitor-analysis.html) suggests it is a widely and strongly felt pain point across the whole category, not a niche concern.

Shortlist

The two gaps to chase first: the estate/health-crisis seller segment paired with a genuinely honoured "the number doesn't move" promise, and explicit Northern Rivers coverage. Both match ground Adam already occupies by fact rather than aspiration, both are structurally hard for the three named direct competitors to chase profitably, and both connect directly to the moves ranked in differentiation-opportunities.html — specifically the "one number, never moved" guarantee, the estate & health-crisis service track, and explicit Northern Rivers coverage.
