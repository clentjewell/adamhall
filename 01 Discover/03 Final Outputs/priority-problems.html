Priority Problems To Solve

How to read this document

Five problems, ranked by impact on the Success Definition (success-definition.html). This is what Design exists to answer — the ranking follows directly from the Discover Summary (discover-summary.html) and every upstream document it distils.

1. The entire conversion system lives only in Adam's head, undocumented

The problem. Valuation judgement, the yes/no instinct, customer-trust technique and payment/settlement discipline are not written down, tested or transferable to anyone else.

Evidence. intelligence-brief-digest.html states plainly that this “makes the business unhireable-into, capacity-capped and unsellable”; sales-process-review.html confirms every one of the eight sales-process steps is currently run by one person; offer-worksheet.html and pricing-packaging-notes.html confirm the valuation decision rules are pending CQ01–CQ03.

Cost of inaction. Capacity stays capped at ~25 cars a month regardless of demand; there is no credible path to $7M without either overworking Adam or documenting the system; the business remains unsellable, directly contradicting the stated 10x/Cars4Us ambition.

Design answer. business-plan.html and growth-roadmap.html (Business & Growth Strategy group), plus the hiring/second-buyer design already scoped in the parallel Jewell Projects engagement.

2. Nothing about the business is measured

The problem. Lead volume, lead source, conversion rates and Adam-hours per deal are all untracked today.

Evidence. funnel-review.html concludes explicitly that “measurement itself is the weakest stage”; sales-process-review.html lists basic lead capture and time/conversion tracking among its top fixes.

Cost of inaction. Every growth and marketing decision in Design and Deploy becomes a guess; there is no way to tell whether reaching $7M needs more leads, better conversion, or more capacity, and no way to prove ROI on any future marketing spend.

Design answer. measurement-plan.html (Business & Growth Strategy group) — basic lead capture and a KPI baseline, instrumented ahead of any paid channel investment.

3. A proven referral behaviour is completely unsystemised

The problem. Customers already refer Adam organically, but there is no formal ask, no tracking and no incentive behind it.

Evidence. funnel-review.html and sales-process-review.html both cite testimonials volunteering unprompted loyalty (“I will only sell my cars to Adam” — Rex, Kyogle) against an unconfirmed referral mechanism (CQ07, CQ16).

Cost of inaction. The cheapest, lowest-risk growth lever available — word of mouth from an already-warm base — is left entirely to chance while budget is instead considered for paid channels with no track record here.

Design answer. crm-plan.html and marketing-plan.html (Sales & Marketing Plan group), informed directly by Adam's own referral-ask wording once CQ16 is answered.

4. Adam's strongest position and the category's sharpest weakness are both unclaimed in market

The problem. The recurring competitor weakness — the price moving after inspection — and Adam's strongest, hardest-to-copy asset — named-person trust plus an implicit estate/health-crisis service — are not yet publicly claimed or proven.

Evidence. competitor-analysis.html documents the recurring ProductReview complaint pattern; differentiation-opportunities.html ranks the “one number, never moved” guarantee and the estate & health-crisis service track as lead and strong second-tier moves; market-gaps.html confirms both gaps are open.

Cost of inaction. Competitors keep occupying price-led positioning by default while Adam's stronger position stays undifferentiated — a real opportunity cost every month the new brand and website aren't live.

Design answer. brand-strategy.html, positioning.html and website-strategy.html (Brand & Messaging and Website & Digital Strategy groups).

5. The brand-naming decision is unresolved and blocks brand and website work

The problem. Whether the brand carries Adam's personal name or becomes independently sellable is still open.

Evidence. USER.html and core-questions-digest.html name CQ11 as “the single biggest open brand decision”; differentiation-opportunities.html explicitly warns against pre-empting it in any differentiation move.

Cost of inaction. Any brand, logo or website work started before this is confirmed risks being rebuilt from scratch, wasting Design-phase time and budget.

Design answer. Not a Design-phase task itself — a Discover→Design gate dependency. CQ11 must be answered by Adam directly before brand-strategy.html or logo-brief.html begin.

Cut line

This engagement will not, within Discover or Design scope: build the vehicle inspection/input tool, the e-commerce marketplace website, or the AI “ask it anything” operating-system portal — all three are confirmed future or separately priced scope per phase1-work-digest.html, not problems this phase is solving now. It will also not invent, assume or publish any HIGH-CONTROL valuation, settlement, PPSR or compliance detail on Adam's behalf — that remains Adam's and the Jewell Projects engagement lead's territory to confirm directly, never Maxxim's to draft from assumption.
