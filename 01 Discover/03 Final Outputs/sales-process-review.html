Time-to-Close and Drop-Off

The only firm timing commitment available publicly is that payment is generally made within 24 hours of a price being agreed. Everything upstream of that — how long between first phone call and the home visit, how many hours the whole deal actually consumes, and how many enquiries never convert to a home visit or a sale — is [NOT AVAILABLE — ask client, pending CQ05]. No drop-off or lost-lead figures are tracked or reported anywhere in the discovery material. This is a genuine measurement gap, not a case of good numbers that simply weren’t shared — see funnel-review.html for what to instrument.

Materials Audit

There is no evidence of a written quote, proposal document, sales deck, or structured follow-up email sequence anywhere in the current process — the valuation is delivered verbally, in person, on the day. The only written customer-facing materials are the website (a WordPress/Elementor brochure site with an FAQ pre-answering the three likely objections: does the valuation cost anything, is there an obligation to sell, and is a particular car even wanted) and whatever minimal paperwork is used at the point of sale, which is itself undocumented pending CQ04. There is no CRM or lead-capture system; lead intake is phone-only, meaning every enquiry’s fate depends on Adam remembering and following it up personally. This absence of any written trail is a real gap, not a minor one, for a $5M business.

Follow-Up Discipline

What happens after a valuation goes quiet — a seller who doesn’t decide on the spot, or a lead who doesn’t answer a call-back — is not described anywhere in the source material. It should not be assumed that a disciplined follow-up habit exists just because the business performs well; the discovery pack explicitly flags the referral ask itself as unconfirmed (“do you ask, or does it occur on its own?” — CQ07), which strongly suggests follow-up more broadly has never been formalised, only carried informally in Adam’s memory alongside everything else.

The 3–5 Fixes

  1. Write down and test the referral ask. Owner: Adam, with Jewell Projects capturing it verbatim during mapping. Highest likely impact for the lowest cost — testimonials already show organic loyalty (“I will only sell my cars to Adam”); the ask itself is the only missing piece.
  2. Introduce basic lead capture, even a simple log. Owner: Adam, or whoever eventually supports him. Removes total dependence on Adam’s memory for tracking who called, what was discussed, and what happened next — the precondition for measuring anything in funnel-review.html.
  3. Start capturing time and conversion data on every deal. Owner: Adam, feeding into the KPI baseline already flagged in the Deploy launch playbook (hours per deal, lead-to-buy conversion, days lead-to-settlement). Nothing about the process can be improved with confidence until this exists.
  4. Document the payment-to-settlement sequence precisely, with Adam. [HIGH-CONTROL] Owner: Jewell Projects engagement lead, working directly with Adam — this must never be drafted from assumption or general knowledge of vehicle sale regulations, only from Adam’s confirmed account (CQ04).
  5. Build toward a second buyer who can run steps 1–7 to the same standard. Owner: Adam, as part of the hiring and onboarding design already scoped for Design phase. This is the structural fix behind every other fix on this list, and the one most likely to break the key-person ceiling — see funnel-review.html for how this affects overall capacity.
