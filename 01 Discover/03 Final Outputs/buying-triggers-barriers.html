Buying Triggers & Barriers

How to read this document

This is the purchase psychology behind the segments in customer-segments.html and the pains ranked in customer-pain-points.html: what starts a seller actively looking for a way to sell, and what stalls or kills the decision once they've found Adam. Most of this is inferred from the confirmed customer profile and the site's own FAQ and trust-signal structure, since none of it has yet been tested with Adam in his own words — treat it as a working hypothesis for messaging and channel timing, not a locked model.

Triggers — what starts the search

1. A life-simplification decision (downsizing)

The seller decides, on their own timeline, to reduce from two cars to one, or to stop driving altogether as part of a broader move to simplify life. Channel moment: this is the calmest trigger — there is time to hear a radio segment, ask a neighbour, or notice a testimonial before acting, making it the best-suited trigger for always-on brand-building channels.

2. Licence revoked or surrendered on medical advice

A licensing authority or medical review forces the decision on a deadline. Channel moment: urgent and narrow — the seller needs an answer within days, not weeks, so word of mouth or a fast, obvious first search result matters more than brand-building content at this exact moment.

3. Death of a family member

An adult child or relative needs to deal with the deceased's car as part of winding up an estate. Channel moment: highly private and emotionally loaded — referral from someone who has been through the same situation is the most credible channel moment, not an ad.

4. A health event that ends someone's driving

A stroke, a diagnosis, a fall, or a general decline that makes driving unsafe, prompting a family member to act on the person's behalf. Channel moment: similar to licence loss but often less externally imposed and more gradual — the family member may search or ask around over a period of weeks before acting.

5. The car sitting unused and becoming a nagging task

No single dramatic event — the car has simply not been driven for a while and its presence becomes a low-grade irritant (registration renewal, insurance, driveway space). Channel moment: a registration renewal notice or an insurance bill is a plausible natural trigger point for this segment. [Inferred, untested.]

6. A bad experience (real or heard about) with private sale or trade-in

The seller, or someone they know, has been burned by a no-show buyer, a lowball trade-in offer, or an online scam attempt, and actively avoids repeating it. Channel moment: word of mouth and reputation matter enormously here — this trigger converts to enquiry fastest when a trusted named alternative is already front of mind.

7. A house move or aged-care transition

Moving to a smaller home, a retirement village, or into aged care forces a decision about what to keep, including the car. Channel moment: often bundled with other decluttering and downsizing decisions — a moment where a broader "sorting out life" mindset makes selling the car feel easier than usual. [Inferred, untested.]

Triggers 5–7 are plausible extensions of the confirmed customer profile, not directly evidenced in source testimonials — worth testing rather than assuming.

Barriers — what stalls or kills the decision

1. "Is the valuation really free?"

Counter: repeat "free, obligation-free valuation" plainly and often, and let the process itself prove it — no pressure to sell once the number is given.

2. "Am I obligated to sell once I get a number?"

Counter: explicit, upfront language that the valuation carries no obligation to sell, reinforced by testimonials describing a no-pressure experience.

3. "Will my car even be wanted?" (older, high-kilometre, unusual)

Counter: make clear the business buys all car types, with a quick, low-friction way to check before committing time to a home visit.

4. Fear of a lowball offer once someone is "committed" to the process

Counter: "Real Market Pricing" framing and testimonials that show a fair number was delivered without pressure to accept it.

5. Distrust of anyone described as a "car buyer" (category-level scepticism)

Counter: lead with Adam as a named, known individual with 27+ years in the trade and a decade of public radio presence — the antidote to category distrust is a specific, verifiable person, not a company name.

6. Not wanting to let a stranger into the home

Counter: testimonials that specifically describe Adam personally attending, and the radio relationship that means many sellers have effectively "met" him before he arrives.

7. Family disagreement over price or timing (estate/health-driven segment)

Counter: a calm, unhurried, no-obligation process gives the family room to agree internally without feeling pushed by the buyer, and clear explanation at each step reduces the chance of second-guessing after the fact. [Inferred counter — not a confirmed script.]

8. Inertia — "I'll get to it eventually"

Counter: lower the perceived effort of starting as far as possible (a single phone call, no need to prepare the car or gather documents first) so the first step feels trivial rather than like the start of a project.

Timing picture

There is no confirmed seasonal pattern in the source material — seasonality is explicitly flagged NOT AVAILABLE, ask client in the underlying discovery dataset. What is visible is a difference in urgency by trigger: downsizing decisions can sit for weeks or months before acting (good fit for always-on brand channels like radio and content), while licence-loss and health-driven triggers compress the buying window to days (best served by fast, frictionless first contact and strong word-of-mouth density, since there is little time for a considered search). Estate-driven sales may follow a slower emotional timeline even though the practical task is simple, meaning patience in follow-up matters more than speed. Confirming any real seasonal pattern (e.g. end-of-financial-year, pre-holiday decluttering, registration renewal cycles) is a gap worth raising with Adam directly.
