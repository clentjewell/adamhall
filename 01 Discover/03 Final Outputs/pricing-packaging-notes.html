Pricing / Packaging Notes

Important framing

These are notes for a partner decision, not a pricing strategy. Nothing here recommends changing what Adam pays a seller or what he charges anyone. Two figures must not be confused: the valuation price Adam offers a seller for their car (individual, unpublished, decided entirely by Adam’s judgement) and the average retail gross of ~$4,200 per car (the margin Adam captures when he resells that car through the retail channel). This document is about the architecture around both, not the numbers behind either. [HIGH-CONTROL adjacent]: the valuation decision rules themselves are pending direct confirmation with Adam (CQ01–CQ03) and are never invented here.

Current Price Architecture

There is no published price, price list or fixed fee anywhere in the business. Every valuation is delivered free, obligation-free, and individually, at the customer’s home — the number is quoted verbally on the day, not published or standardised. Low-kilometre, later-model cars are preferred; some cars are declined upfront as unsuitable, rather than being quoted a low-ball figure. At the business level, the only confirmed aggregate figures are: ~25 cars bought per month (range 20–30), a ~90% retail / 10% wholesale split, and an average retail gross of ~$4,200 per car. There is no evidence of a published discounting habit, because there is no published price to discount from — every price is already individually quoted.

Anchors and Comparisons

A seller considering this service is likely comparing it, consciously or not, against three alternatives: selling privately online (Facebook Marketplace, Gumtree, classifieds-style sites), trading in at a dealership, or using another instant-offer “we buy any car”-type buying service. [Categories only — no named competitor has been researched yet; see competitor-analysis.html for that work once it exists.]

The business does not appear to anchor on price at all — there is no pricing shown anywhere on the site, consistent with a valuation-led, trust-first model. The anchor being sold instead is certainty and safety: no scam risk, no no-show buyers, no unexplained trade-in number, a known and named person standing behind the offer. This is a deliberate, and currently effective, alternative to price anchoring — but it also means the business has no visibility into how its unpublished prices compare to what competitors or private sale would realistically deliver, because that competitive pricing picture doesn’t exist yet.

What the Market Supports

There is no won/lost deal data, no competitor pricing benchmark and no documented buyer reaction data available to assess headroom or pressure on pricing. The discovery data confirms unit economics work today (~$4,200 average retail gross sustained across ~25 cars/month), but says nothing about whether that gross could be higher, lower, or is already close to what the market and the trust-first positioning will bear. [Inference, flagged as inference, not evidence]: a trust-first, older audience that values certainty over the last dollar plausibly tolerates a fair-but-not-maximised price, which is consistent with the business choosing reputation over squeezing every deal — but this has not been tested and should not be treated as licence to move pricing in either direction without evidence.

Packaging Options

Today there is one package: the free valuation leading to a purchase, full stop. Three packaging directions exist as hypotheses only, drawn from the Design-phase offer synthesis, and are flagged here for the partner to test with Adam, not to adopt:

  • Entry / commercial spine / LTV structure. Entry: the free home-visit valuation as the universal lead-in. Commercial spine: the retail purchase itself, carrying the ~$4,200 gross. LTV engine: referrals and repeat family transactions, treated as a distinct, nameable stage rather than an accident. This mirrors what offer-worksheet.html already proposes as the offer structure to test.
  • Speed-tiered service (untested idea). A standard 24-hour payment path alongside a possible expedited or guaranteed same-day path, if operationally feasible — flagged purely as a concept to explore, not a commitment, and dependent on answering how much slack exists in Adam’s current capacity (CQ05, CQ21).
  • Retail / wholesale as a named, transparent choice. Rather than the 90/10 split being an invisible internal routing decision, it could become a documented, explainable rule the customer is told about (“your car suits our retail channel / our wholesale channel, and here’s why”) — this depends entirely on first documenting the routing rule itself, which does not yet exist (CQ03).

None of these should be built or communicated to customers until tested and confirmed with Adam.
