Stage Map

Traffic sources. The dominant hypothesised source is Adam’s decade-long radio relationship — “What’s Your Car Worth?” on 4CRB 89.3FM, with over 10,000 live valuations delivered on air — alongside organic word of mouth and referral. The website (adamhallbuymycar.com.au) exists as a supporting, brochure-style presence, not a lead-generation engine; there is no evidence of paid digital or social advertising history. [Channel mix is hypothesis only, pending CQ06 — the confirmed lead-source split does not yet exist.]

Capture. Every lead currently arrives as a phone call to Adam directly. There is no digital lead form, no CRM and no structured intake record — capture is entirely manual and entirely dependent on Adam personally answering and remembering each enquiry.

Nurture. There is effectively no nurture stage today: the model moves from first contact almost straight to a home-visit valuation, with no evidence of an email sequence, retargeting, or a considered follow-up cadence for anyone who doesn’t proceed immediately.

Conversion. Conversion happens at the home visit: Adam values the car, the seller decides, and if they agree, the sale, paperwork, payment and pick-up follow in short order. This stage is well-proven in outcome (~25 cars bought a month) but entirely undocumented in method — see sales-process-review.html for the step-by-step detail.

Repeat/referral. Testimonials suggest organic loyalty and word-of-mouth referral already occur (“I will only sell my cars to Adam”), but there is no formal ask, no tracking and no incentive behind it — see the Repeat & Referral section below.

The Weakest Stage

The evidence does not point to a demand problem: ~25 cars a month, sustained on trust alone with apparently no paid acquisition spend, says top-of-funnel demand and conversion at the point of sale both already work. The honest answer to “which stage is weakest” is that measurement itself is the weakest stage — with no tracked numbers between “lead in” and “car bought,” it is not possible to say with evidence whether capture, nurture or conversion is where value actually leaks, only that nothing is currently instrumented to show it.

The strongest evidenced structural weakness is not a funnel stage at all but a hard capacity ceiling: every lead, valuation, decision and settlement currently routes through one person’s calendar, which caps monthly volume at roughly 25 cars regardless of how much demand exists upstream. This is a conversion-and-capacity issue, not a top-of-funnel one — the fixes for it are detailed in sales-process-review.html, principally building a second buyer who can run the process to the same standard.

What to Instrument

  1. Lead source tracking (radio vs. word-of-mouth vs. website vs. other) — unlocks the decision of where to actually invest marketing effort, rather than assuming radio leads on hypothesis alone.
  2. Lead-to-home-visit and home-visit-to-sale conversion rates — unlocks whether the real leak (if any) is in qualification or in the valuation conversation itself.
  3. Referral rate and referral source — unlocks whether the LTV engine hypothesised in the offer architecture is real, and how big a lever formalising the ask could be.
  4. Adam-hours per deal and days lead-to-settlement — unlocks the true monthly capacity ceiling and how close the business already is to it, which directly determines whether growth to $7M is even possible under the current model.
  5. Marketing spend and outcome by channel — unlocks an honest read on what, if anything, is actually paying for itself today, ahead of any new spend decision in Deploy.

Repeat & Referral

Existing customers plausibly do generate the next leads: several testimonials volunteer strong, unprompted loyalty (“I will only sell my cars to Adam” — Rex, Kyogle) and the family-referral pattern shows up repeatedly (an adult child managing a parent’s car, a neighbour or estate contact passing the name along). [Pattern inferred from testimonial content and discovery hypothesis, not confirmed as a measured behaviour by Adam.]

However, there is no formal ask, no tracking and no incentive structure behind any of this today — the discovery pack states plainly that whether Adam asks for a referral, or it simply happens on its own, is unconfirmed (CQ07). The cheapest available lever for volume growth without new spend is systemising this single behaviour: a simple, in-his-own-words referral ask (CQ16), asked consistently and tracked from the first instance, rather than left to chance.
