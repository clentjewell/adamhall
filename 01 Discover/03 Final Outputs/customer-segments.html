Customer Segments

How to read this document

Four segments sit inside the 60–75+ trust-first customer base described in audience-teardown.html: three named directly in the discovery material, plus one adjacent, higher-value niche visible in the site's own product range but not yet segment-tested. None of these have been confirmed segment-by-segment with Adam for volume, margin or referral behaviour — treat the ranking and detail below as an informed hypothesis to validate, not a locked customer list. Pain detail for each segment is kept brief here; the full ranked pain stack lives in customer-pain-points.html.

Segment 1 — Downsizers

Who they are (plain English). Retirees or near-retirees reducing from two cars to one, or voluntarily giving up driving, usually as part of a broader life simplification. Primary goal: sell the extra or unneeded car with as little fuss as possible, for a fair price, without becoming a project.

Excruciating pain. Fear of being ripped off combined with dread of the private-sale process itself — strangers, paperwork, technology. See customer-pain-points.html for the full evidenced ranking.

Purchasing power. Full control of the decision — this is their own car and their own call, made without needing anyone else's sign-off. No fixed range; car values vary widely, and this segment transacts once every few years at most, so it is a low-frequency, high-trust-dependency sale rather than a repeat-purchase relationship.

Easy to target and engage. Reachable through channels this audience already trusts — local radio (4CRB), community networks, word of mouth — but not yet reachable in a measured, repeatable way; the actual channel mix and share is unconfirmed. [Pending CQ06.]

Riches in niches. This segment is currently served well by trust but poorly by discovery — there is no structured way for a downsizer who hasn't heard the radio segment or doesn't know anyone who has used Adam to find him. Closing that discovery gap, rather than adding a new offer, is the underserved opportunity here.

The transformation. Before: an extra car sitting unused, a nagging sense it should be sold, and dread of the process required to do it. After: the car is gone, the money is in the account, and the whole thing took one visit and one phone call — with no negotiation and no strangers traipsing through the driveway.

Best channels.

  • 4CRB radio ("What's Your Car Worth?") — the strongest known trust-building touchpoint for this age group
  • Word-of-mouth and referral from a friend who has already sold to Adam
  • Community and local-area Facebook groups, particularly seniors' and neighbourhood groups
  • Direct mail or letterbox drop in target suburbs, given this audience's lower digital-first behaviour [untested idea]

Messaging angles.

  • "No haggling, no strangers — just a fair number, at your door"
  • "You don't have to deal with this on your own"
  • "27 years, one name you already know from the radio"
  • "Free valuation, no obligation to sell"

Segment 2 — Licence-loss sellers

Who they are (plain English). Sellers who must sell soon, usually because a driving licence has been revoked or surrendered on medical advice. Primary goal: sell quickly, fairly and without the situation feeling exposed or humiliating.

Excruciating pain. Urgency colliding with pride — needing to sell fast while fearing that urgency will be read as desperation and exploited on price. See customer-pain-points.html.

Purchasing power. Full control of the decision, though the timeline is often externally forced (a medical review date, a licence-authority deadline) rather than chosen by the seller.

Easy to target and engage. Harder to reach proactively than downsizers, because the trigger is a private medical or licensing event with no obvious public channel. Referral from someone who has been through the same situation, or from a health professional involved in the licence review, is the most plausible path — neither is currently systemised. [Hypothesis — untested channel.]

Riches in niches. A genuinely underserved need: nobody appears to be marketing specifically to the moment a licence is lost, despite it being a sharply defined, high-intent trigger. A light touch here (without being insensitive about the loss involved) could capture demand competitors aren't targeting at all.

The transformation. Before: anxious, time-pressured, worried about being taken advantage of because "I have to sell now." After: sold within days, for a number that felt fair, without having to explain or justify the situation to a stranger.

Best channels.

  • Word of mouth from others who have been through licence loss
  • 4CRB radio, for general awareness ahead of the trigger moment
  • Potential referral relationships with GPs, optometrists or driving assessment services [untested, not yet built]

Messaging angles.

  • "Sell this week, without the stress"
  • "No judgement, no drawn-out process — just a fair, fast outcome"
  • "We come to you, so you don't have to arrange anything"

Segment 3 — Estate or health-driven family sellers

Who they are (plain English). Usually an adult child or close family member selling a car on behalf of a parent or relative who has died or can no longer manage the process due to illness. Primary goal: handle the car respectfully and efficiently, as one task among many during an already difficult time.

Excruciating pain. The emotional weight of the situation stacked on top of a practical task neither party has the bandwidth for. See customer-pain-points.html.

Purchasing power. Acts with delegated authority — can say yes on behalf of the actual owner, which removes one layer of friction other segments don't have but adds the responsibility of getting it right for someone else.

Easy to target and engage. The hardest segment to reach proactively, since the trigger is private and often sudden. Referral is the most credible channel — someone who has been through the same situation recommending Adam to a friend or colleague going through it now. [Inferred from testimonial pattern, e.g. the Currumbin Waters testimonial — not confirmed as a general pattern with Adam.]

Riches in niches. This is the segment where a wrong touch (too salesy, too transactional) does the most damage, and where the right touch (patient, human, no pressure) creates the strongest advocacy — a family who felt properly cared for during a hard moment is a powerful, values-aligned referral source, not just a one-off sale.

The transformation. Before: one more difficult task in a season of difficult tasks, with the added worry of doing right by someone else's car and someone else's money. After: handled properly, respectfully and quickly by someone who came to them, freeing them to focus on the family situation rather than the paperwork.

Best channels.

  • Referral from friends, neighbours or colleagues who have used the service in a similar situation
  • Word of mouth generally — this segment is unlikely to be actively searching before the moment arrives
  • Possible aged-care, allied-health or funeral-service adjacent networks [untested, sensitive to get right]

Messaging angles.

  • "We'll take care of it, so you don't have to"
  • "Handled with the same care Adam would want for his own family"
  • "One call, and it's off your list"

Segment 4 — Premium and low-kilometre vehicle owners (emerging niche)

Who they are (plain English). Owners of later-model, low-kilometre or premium/luxury vehicles (the website cites a Maserati example) who want a discreet, no-fuss sale and are less price-sensitive than the other three segments, but equally unwilling to deal with strangers or online marketplaces for a valuable car. Primary goal: a confidential, professional transaction that matches the calibre of the car.

Excruciating pain. Reluctance to expose a high-value car to public marketplace listings (safety, privacy, tyre-kickers) and a shortage of buyers who can transact quickly at the right level of professionalism. [Hypothesis — this segment is visible in the offer ("buys all car types... including luxury brands") but not named or tested as a distinct segment anywhere in the discovery material.]

Purchasing power. Likely higher average transaction value than the other three segments, which would lift average retail gross if this niche is deliberately pursued — but this is not quantified and needs testing against real transaction data.

Easy to target and engage. Currently the least understood segment — no dedicated messaging, channel or proof point targets it specifically today.

Riches in niches. Potentially the highest-margin-per-transaction niche the business already touches without positioning for it. Worth testing deliberately once volume and margin data can confirm whether it is worth a dedicated push.

The transformation. Before: hesitant to list a valuable car publicly, unsure who to trust with a bigger sale. After: a private, professional transaction handled by someone whose reputation and 27 years of trade experience match the value of the car.

Best channels and messaging angles. Not yet developed — flagged here as a segment worth a dedicated look once Adam confirms whether this is a segment he wants to grow or a byproduct of an inclusive buying policy. [NOT AVAILABLE — ask client.]

Priority call

Aim the engagement first at Downsizers and Estate/health-driven family sellers. Downsizers are the largest, calmest, most reachable segment and the best fit for building repeatable, always-on channels (radio, community, referral) without needing a crisis trigger to convert. Estate/health-driven sellers are smaller in volume but carry the highest advocacy value — get the tone right here and it compounds into referral for all three other segments, since this is the segment most likely to talk about the experience, not just the transaction. Licence-loss sellers remain a real, high-intent segment worth capturing opportunistically, but building a dedicated acquisition channel for a private, hard-to-predict trigger is lower priority until CQ06 (lead source mix) is answered. The premium/low-kilometre niche should stay a watch item — worth a deliberate test once Adam confirms appetite and margin, not a day-one priority.
