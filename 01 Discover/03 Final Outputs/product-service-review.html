Product / Service Review

The Line-up

This is an unusually thin line-up for a $5M-revenue business, and that thinness is itself the headline finding. There is effectively one customer-facing service and two internal resale channels behind it — not a portfolio of products.

ItemWhat it isWho it’s forWhat it does for them
Free home-visit valuation The customer-facing lead offer: Adam attends the seller’s home and gives a free, obligation-free, expert opinion on the car’s worth Any car owner in the service area considering a sale, especially an anxious or time-poor seller Removes the risk and hassle of finding out what the car is worth; costs nothing and carries no obligation
Retail purchase (~90% of volume) Adam buys the car outright and resells it through the retail channel Sellers of low-kilometre, later-model cars that suit resale Carries the disclosed ~$4,200 average retail gross — the business’s main margin engine
Wholesale purchase (~10% of volume) Adam buys cars that don’t suit retail and moves them on wholesale Sellers of older, higher-kilometre or otherwise less desirable cars that Adam still chooses to buy Lets Adam still say yes to a seller he’d otherwise have to turn away, at presumably thinner margin
Decline (unsuitable cars) Cars Adam judges not worth buying at all are declined upfront Sellers of cars outside what the service wants to buy Saves the seller’s time rather than stringing them along — a trust-building act, even though it’s a “no”

Everything else referenced in the engagement — the e-commerce marketplace website, the vehicle inspection/input tool, the AI operating-system portal — is scoped future work, not a live product today. It should not be described as part of the current line-up.

Margin & Effort Signals

The only disclosed, confirmed margin figure is the ~$4,200 average retail gross per car on the retail channel, which carries roughly 90% of the ~25 cars bought per month. Effort to deliver the retail channel is high and entirely manual: a personal home visit, a judgement-based valuation, on-the-spot paperwork, payment and pick-up, all run by one person. It is repeatable in the sense that it happens ~25 times a month, but not repeatable in the sense of being teachable — no one else can currently run this process end to end.

Wholesale margin per car is [NOT AVAILABLE — ask client]; it is reasonable to assume it runs thinner than retail (that is the usual reason a car is routed wholesale rather than retail), but this is an estimate, not a disclosed figure, and should not be quoted as fact. The rule that sorts a car into retail versus wholesale is itself undocumented — it is a gut call, not a written rule (CQ03 pending) — so even the 90/10 split should be read as an outcome of Adam’s judgement, not a designed policy.

Carrying vs. Coasting

The retail channel carries the business outright: it is 90% of volume and the only channel with a confirmed margin figure. Word-of-mouth reputation and the testimonials on the website suggest it also carries the business’s referral pipeline, though that pipeline is not measured (see funnel-review.html).

The wholesale channel is closer to coasting: it exists to avoid saying a flat “no” to sellers whose cars don’t suit retail, which may have goodwill value, but it is not shown to carry revenue, referrals or reputation in any measured way. It should not be assumed to be a strategic growth lever without evidence.

The “decline” behaviour (turning away unsuitable cars upfront) is worth naming separately: it isn’t revenue-generating, but the website treats it as a trust signal (“Adam will say so upfront rather than waste your time”), which plausibly protects the reputation that the whole retail channel depends on.

Kill / Fix / Double Down

  • Double down — the retail home-visit purchase. It is the only proven, margin-carrying, high-repeat item in the line-up, and it is what every testimonial on the site is actually praising.
  • Fix — the wholesale routing rule. Not because wholesale is broken, but because the decision that sends a car there instead of retail is undocumented judgement, not a written rule; fixing this is a documentation problem before it is a pricing problem (see pricing-packaging-notes.html).
  • Fix — the referral mechanism. Multiple testimonials volunteer loyalty (“I will only sell my cars to Adam”) but there is no formal ask, tracking or incentive behind it — a proven behaviour is going unsystemised (see funnel-review.html).
  • Kill — nothing. The line-up is too thin to have anything worth killing; the finding here is concentration risk, not clutter. A single person, running a single process, is the entire commercial engine.

Gaps and Adjacencies

The most obvious gap customers are not asked to fill today is a documented second point of contact: everything from valuation to payment routes through Adam alone, so there is no adjacency yet that reduces that single point of failure. The engagement’s own scope already names the right next additions — a vehicle inspection/input tool feeding a proper inventory system, and an e-commerce marketplace website — both of which are future-build items, not gaps this review is raising fresh.

The adjacency with the best near-term fit is formalising the referral pathway: adult children, neighbours and estate contacts are already becoming the next lead by word of mouth (per testimonials and the discovery hypothesis), but nothing captures or rewards that behaviour today. This is a low-cost addition to the existing line-up rather than a new product, and it is the fastest lever available without new spend.
