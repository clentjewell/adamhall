Audience Teardown

Market at a glance

Three main markets

1. Downsizers. Older owners voluntarily reducing to one car, or giving up driving by choice rather than by crisis. This is the calmest, most planned segment — appetite is steady and the sales process has the most room to breathe. Winnable now through warm, unhurried messaging rather than urgency.

2. Licence-loss sellers. Sellers who must sell fast, often on a deadline set by a medical or licensing authority, and with dignity intact. Smaller in any given month than downsizers but high-intent the moment they appear — the size signal is a steady trickle rather than a wave, and appetite is immediate once triggered.

3. Estate or health-driven sellers. Typically an adult child or family member selling a parent's or relative's car after a death or a health event that ends someone's driving. Emotionally the heaviest segment and the one most likely to need a human, not a marketplace, at the point of contact. Market awareness

Buyers in this market are highly aware of the underlying problem (an unwanted or unneeded car that has to go) but largely unaware there is a named, trusted alternative to a private sale or a dealer trade-in. The category itself — "we buy your car" services — has low trust by reputation (instant-offer aggregators, lowball online quotes), which works in Adam's favour once he is discovered, but means the discovery moment matters more than the pitch. Awareness of Adam Hall specifically is believed to run through two channels: the 4CRB radio relationship ("What's Your Car Worth?", 10,000+ live valuations on air since 2010) and word of mouth/referral. Growing, on current signal. An ageing population is expanding the pool of downsizers and licence-loss sellers year on year, and rising distrust of online marketplace scams and no-show private buyers pushes older sellers toward a known, human alternative rather than away from it. This matters now because the business's stated growth path ($5M to $7M, then a longer 10x path toward a Cars For Us-style exit) depends on this audience continuing to grow and on Adam's judgement becoming transferable enough to serve more of it than one calendar allows. Define your niche

Target audience

Sellers aged roughly 60–75 and older, across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers, who are not trying to extract the last dollar from a private sale — they want the sale handled by someone they can trust, with as little friction, technology and stranger-contact as possible. Decision-making sits with the seller directly for downsizers and licence-loss sellers, and with an adult child or family member acting on the seller's behalf for the estate/health-driven segment. [Inferred from D06/DS hypothesis — not yet segment-confirmed with Adam.]

Problem they need solved

Selling a car privately or trading it in is a slog for this audience: cleaning and detailing the car, fielding lowball offers, arranging inspections with strangers who don't show up, and doing it all through apps and marketplaces that assume comfort with technology this audience may not have. For the estate/health-driven segment, the problem is compounded — the car is a reminder of a loss or a decline, and the family member selling it is already stretched.

Current behaviour

Today's alternatives are: list privately on Facebook Marketplace, Gumtree or a classifieds site and manage the whole process solo; trade in at a dealership and accept a below-market number for convenience; or do nothing and let the car sit, unsold, because the effort feels too high relative to the reward. None of these give this audience what it actually wants, which is certainty and a person to trust, not a price to negotiate.

Unique approach

Adam attaches trust to a known, named individual with 27+ years in the trade and a decade of public radio credibility, not to an anonymous buyer or a call centre. The valuation happens at the seller's home — removing the two hardest parts of the current alternatives (driving the car anywhere, meeting a stranger) — and is free and obligation-free. If the seller agrees, payment follows same-day or next-day via OSKO, and pick-up is free. This is a proven, valued difference, evidenced directly in testimonials ("I will only sell my cars to Adam" — Rex, Kyogle).

Why now

Two forces make this timely: an ageing population is producing more downsizing, licence-loss and estate sellers each year, and growing wariness of online marketplace scams is actively pushing this audience away from the DIY alternatives and toward a known human buyer. Separately, the confirmed engagement scope includes building the next website to be visible to AI chatbots and generative search, not just traditional search — a real "why now" for how this audience will find Adam next, since older sellers increasingly ask an AI assistant a question rather than typing a search term. [Inferred from D06/DD00 hypothesis and the confirmed AI-visibility scope item in the Jewell Projects engagement.]

High-conversion readiness

Problem-aware: yes, strongly — the car is a known, present decision, not a hypothetical. Budget-ready: not applicable in the traditional sense, since these are sellers, not buyers, but "ready to transact" is high once contact is made — the free, no-obligation frame removes the main reason to stall. Hungry for change: high for licence-loss and estate/health-driven sellers (the trigger event has already forced the decision); moderate-to-building for downsizers, who may sit with the decision for a while before acting. The main conversion barrier is not desire, it is discovery — this audience needs to find Adam before any of this readiness can convert.

Persona targeting

Persona 1 — Barbara, 71, the downsizer

Retired, living in a Gold Coast hinterland town, has decided to go from two cars to one now that her husband no longer drives. Not in a hurry, but wants the process to feel dignified, not transactional.

Pain points. Fear of being taken advantage of because she doesn't know what the car is really worth; dread of dealing with unknown buyers arranging inspections at her home; not wanting to manage a listing, photos or messages from strangers.

Dream state. A fair number, explained simply, delivered by someone she can trust, with the car gone and the money in her account within a day or two — no negotiation, no chasing.

Buying power. Full decision-maker — she owns the car and makes the call herself, though she may mention it to an adult child for reassurance before committing.

Where to find her. Local radio (4CRB), community word of mouth, and increasingly a voice or chat search when she has a question rather than a Google search box. [Inferred — channel mix not yet confirmed with Adam, pending CQ06.]

Persona 2 — Graham, 78, the licence-loss seller

Recently had his driving licence revoked or voluntarily surrendered on medical advice. Needs to sell his car soon, is proud, and does not want the situation broadcast or fussed over.

Pain points. Urgency without wanting to look desperate; the emotional loss of independence tangled up with the practical task of selling; anxiety about being lowballed because he "has to sell."

Dream state. A quick, respectful, no-drama sale that lets him move on from this chapter without it becoming a bigger event than it needs to be.

Buying power. Full decision-maker, though family may be involved in the background, especially around the licence situation itself.

Where to find him. Word of mouth from someone who has been through the same licence-loss situation; radio; possibly a referral from a GP, optometrist or driving assessment service. [Inferred — no confirmed referral-partner channel exists yet; this is a testable idea, not a validated channel.]

Persona 3 — Jen, 46, the family seller

Adult daughter managing her elderly father's affairs after a health decline. Selling his car is one task among many, and she is doing it on his behalf, often while he is unwell or has already moved into care. [Modelled directly on the site's own Currumbin Waters testimonial: "My father is unwell and finds it difficult to leave the house. Dad was so grateful Adam was able to help."]

Pain points. No time or emotional bandwidth to manage a private sale; guilt or grief tangled up with a practical task; needing someone who will come to them rather than expecting the family to organise transport or inspections.

Dream state. One phone call, one visit, one straightforward outcome — the car handled properly and respectfully so she can focus on her father, not the paperwork.

Buying power. Acts as decision-maker on behalf of the actual owner — she can say yes without needing her father present for every step, which is itself part of what she values.

Where to find her. Referral from a friend or colleague who has been through something similar; possibly aged-care or allied-health networks; general search when researching "how to sell a parent's car." [Inferred — not a confirmed channel.]
