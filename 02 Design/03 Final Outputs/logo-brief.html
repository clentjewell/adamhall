Logo Brief

1. The mark in one line

A mark that feels like a handshake at the front door — instantly warm and personal today under Adam’s own name, and built so its symbol can carry “The Adam Hall Standard” forward on its own if the brand’s name ever needs to generalise.

2. Brand context

Adam Hall Buy My Car is a home-visit, direct car-buying service across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers, for sellers aged 60–75 and older who want relief and trust rather than a negotiation. The brand’s personality is The Caregiver (primary) and The Everyman (secondary) — warm, protective, grounded and plain-spoken, never corporate or blunt. It claims the trust-and-ease-maximising, fully human corner of a category where every named competitor is a company, a branch network or a dealer panel, not a person — and its sharpest proof asset is Adam himself: 27+ years in the trade and a decade hosting “What’s Your Car Worth?” on 4CRB 89.3FM. Full detail in brand-strategy.html and positioning.html.

3. What the logo must do

Communication goals. Signal warmth, trust and personal continuity at a glance — the opposite of a dealership or a tech platform. It must read as calm and grounded, like a trusted local adviser, never like a “sale” sign or a fintech app. It must not lean on category clichés (see Constraints, below).

Functional requirements.

  • Legible at favicon and app-icon size (down to roughly 32px) and at social-avatar size, including a circular crop.
  • Reproduces cleanly in a single flat colour — no gradients, no photographic textures, no 3D/bevel effects.
  • Holds up reversed on a dark background (the primary Harbour Navy from brand-guidelines.html).
  • Reads clearly at distance on vehicle signage and at small size on a printed flyer, business card and letterhead.
  • Works as a co-branded or credit-line element alongside the 4CRB radio association without visually competing with it.

These contexts — website header, favicon/app icon, social avatar, vehicle signage/decal, printed flyer, letterhead, email signature — become the variant set once a human designer refines the chosen Studio candidate.

4. Form & concept direction

Form language. Warm and grounded geometry — not the sharp, aggressive angles of a dealership speed-mark, and not an overly rounded, bubbly, “friendly cartoon” treatment either. Moderate, confident stroke weight; generous negative space; quiet symmetry rather than dynamic diagonal energy. No gradients, no 3D, no photographic effects in the mark itself.

Type of mark to pursue: a combination mark — a distinct symbol paired with a wordmark, deliberately not a wordmark-only lockup. This is a direct, load-bearing decision from brand-strategy.html’s Visual Identity Direction: because the brand-naming question (CQ11) is unresolved, a strong, independent symbol is what allows the mark to survive a future name change without a full rebuild. A purely typographic “Adam Hall” lockup with no separate symbol cannot do this.

Concept territories to explore (pick two or three to generate against):

  • A — The doorstep mark. An abstracted symbol built from the idea of arriving at someone’s home — a simplified doorway, threshold or handshake gesture, reduced to two or three clean strokes. Carries “we come to you” directly, without a single automotive cliché.
  • B — The steady dial. A symbol referencing the warmth of analogue radio — a simple dial, needle or soundwave arc, reduced to its simplest form. Nods to the 4CRB “What’s Your Car Worth?” credibility asset without becoming a literal radio icon.
  • C — The Standard seal. A confident, simple monogram or seal-like mark built from the initials “AH”, designed from the outset to also work alone, later, as a certification-style mark for “The Adam Hall Standard” — the recommended staged-transition path (see Colour direction and Brand context, above, and Section 9 below).

Device to carry forward or retire. The website crawl (website-crawl-digest.html) captured no distinct existing logo mark or favicon treatment beyond the plain wordmark “Adam Hall” used as a page title — there is no strong existing device on record to preserve. Treat this as a from-scratch brief rather than a refinement brief, but confirm directly with Adam whether any existing physical signage, vehicle decals or printed materials carry a mark not captured in the crawl before finalising a direction.

5. Colour direction

Provisional — not yet settled. No hex values are locked in until Brand Identity confirms them; the Studio should treat the following as its starting palette, carried directly from brand-guidelines.html:

  • Harbour Navy #1E4A5F — primary; calm, grounded, trustworthy.
  • Harbour Navy Deep #163846 — for the inverse/reversed plate on dark backgrounds.
  • Brass Trust #B8863E — secondary warmth accent, used sparingly.

Psychological direction. Calm, grounded and trustworthy — closer to a respected local tradesperson or financial adviser than a car dealership. Explicitly avoid the loud reds, yellows and “sale!” urgency colours the used-car category defaults to (visible on CarBuyers’ and We Buy Cars’ sites, per competitor-analysis.html) — those colours signal exactly the price-led, high-pressure category this brand exists to sit apart from.

6. Typography direction (wordmark / lockup)

Pairing direction, carried from brand-guidelines.html: the wordmark should be set in Lora (a warm, well-proportioned serif with a personal, “letter from someone you trust” feel), with any supporting tagline or descriptor line set in PT Sans for maximum legibility at small sizes.

Lockups required so the Studio builds a system, not just a single mark:

  • Horizontal lockup — symbol beside the wordmark, side by side. Primary use: website header, letterhead, email signature.
  • Stacked lockup — symbol above the wordmark. Primary use: social avatar, app icon, narrow vehicle-signage panels where width is constrained.
  • Icon-only lockup — the symbol alone, no wordmark. Primary use: favicon, app icon at very small sizes, social avatar crop.

7. Tone & references

Emotional territory. Like a respected small-town GP, a trusted financial adviser, or a family-run trade business three generations in — calm competence and personal accountability, not salesmanship.

Feel like (qualitative territory, not literal copies): the calm confidence of a well-established local law or accounting firm; the restrained trustworthiness of a heritage regional bank; the warm familiarity of a community radio station.

Explicitly avoid:

  • Car dealership visual clichés — chrome effects, checkered flags, speed lines, aggressive angular “fast” marks, screeching-tyre motifs.
  • Red/yellow/orange “SALE” colour schemes and starburst or exclamation-mark treatments.
  • The generic “we buy any car!” van-signage look.
  • Cold, minimalist fintech-app aesthetics — this brand is a person, not a platform, and the mark should never read as a tech start-up logo.

8. Constraints & anti-patterns

  • Scalability. Must remain legible from a 32px favicon up to a full vehicle-signage panel.
  • Single-colour reproduction. Must work as a flat, single-colour mark with no loss of meaning — no gradients, drop shadows, 3D bevels or photographic textures.
  • Reversal. Must have a confirmed inverse/reversed treatment for use on Harbour Navy or another dark ground.
  • Circular crop. Must remain legible and balanced when cropped to a circle for social avatar use.
  • Category clichés to steer clear of: car silhouettes, keys, steering wheels, tyres or any literal automotive iconography; aggressive “urgent” visual language; anything that could sit comfortably on a used-car lot banner.

9. Deliverables & definition of done

The Design Studio produces multiple candidates across the concept territories in Section 4. The partner (Adam, via Maxxim) reviews and picks one direction. A human designer then refines the chosen candidate into the full variant set — primary, mono, inverse, brandmark/icon — plus the horizontal, stacked and icon-only lockups specified in Section 6. The Studio candidate is a candidate, not the final mark — it does not ship to a customer-facing surface until refined and confirmed.

Once that refinement is complete, the settled palette, typography and logo files supersede the provisional versions currently in brand-guidelines.html, and that document should be reissued as the canonical, settled record.

The CQ11 decision gate. This brief deliberately briefs the recommended Scenario C staged-transition path from brand-strategy.html: build and use the mark under the current personal-name brand (“Adam Hall Buy My Car”) now, while constructing it — specifically via the combination-mark decision in Section 4 — so its symbol can carry “The Adam Hall Standard” forward independently later, if and when Adam decides to generalise the brand beyond his personal name. This is a recommendation, not a decision. CQ11 itself — whether the brand permanently carries Adam’s name, generalises fully, or stages the transition as recommended here — has not been put to Adam and must not be treated as resolved by this brief or by anything the Studio generates from it. No permanent, name-locked asset (a registered trademark, a domain name, vehicle-signage tooling with a long replacement cycle) should be finalised from this brief’s output until CQ11 is answered directly with Adam.

Deep muted navy-teal (#1E4A5F) and warm brass (#B8863E) on a warm off-white background. Calm, trustworthy and grounded — like a respected local adviser, not a car dealership. No car silhouettes, no chrome, no gradients; must reproduce as a flat single colour.”
  • “A simple analogue radio-dial or soundwave-arc symbol, reduced to two or three clean strokes, paired with the wordmark ‘Adam Hall Buy My Car’ in a warm serif typeface — referencing a trusted community radio voice rather than an automotive icon. Same deep navy (#1E4A5F) and brass (#B8863E) palette, flat colour reproduction, legible down to favicon size.”
  • “A confident, simple monogram or seal mark built from the initials ‘AH’, designed so it can also stand alone later as a certification-style mark for ‘The Adam Hall Standard’. Warm navy and brass palette, flat single colour, geometric but not cold or corporate, legible at 32px, with a clear reversed/inverse version on a dark navy ground.”
