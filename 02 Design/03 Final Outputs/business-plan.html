Business Plan

How to read this document

This is the umbrella commercial analysis for Adam Hall Buy My Car, opening the Design phase's Business & Growth Strategy group. It grounds everything else Design produces — brand, website, marketing — in commercial reality, built on the fourteen approved Discover documents in memory/generated/discover/, especially discover-summary.html, success-definition.html, objectives-key-results.html and priority-problems.html. Three sibling documents carry the depth this plan only headlines: the full case for each bet lives in strategic-priorities.html, the staged execution path in growth-roadmap.html, and the complete metric tree in measurement-plan.html.

Two facts shape every section below. First, none of Adam's 22 CORE questions have been answered yet (core-questions-digest.html), so every audience, offer and valuation hypothesis in the Discover documents remains unvalidated until Adam confirms it directly — this plan treats those hypotheses as hypotheses, not facts. Second, anything touching money, settlement, PPSR or compliance is HIGH-CONTROL: confirmed with Adam directly, never invented here.

Business model summary

Value proposition. A free, obligation-free home-visit valuation, followed — if the seller agrees — by an immediate direct purchase, payment generally within 24 hours by OSKO transfer, and free pick-up. The transformation on offer is relief from the private-sale slog and dealership trade-in uncertainty, delivered by one named, known person rather than a call centre or dealer panel. Full detail in offer-worksheet.html.

Customer segments. Predominantly 60–75+ sellers across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers, trust-first and price-second. Three named segments carry the priority call: Downsizers (largest, calmest, most reachable) and Estate/health-driven family sellers (smaller, highest advocacy value) are the two the engagement should lead with; Licence-loss sellers are a real but harder-to-target opportunistic segment; a fourth, premium/low-kilometre niche is visible in the offer but untested as a distinct segment. Full detail in audience-teardown.html and customer-segments.html. [Segments are an informed hypothesis pending Adam's confirmation, not yet volume- or margin-tested.]

Channels today. A decade-long relationship with 4CRB 89.3FM radio ("What's Your Car Worth?", 10,000+ live on-air valuations since 2010) and organic word of mouth are the dominant hypothesised lead sources. The website is a five-page brochure site, not a lead-generation engine. There is no paid digital or social advertising history and no CRM — every lead arrives as a phone call to Adam personally. [Channel mix is hypothesis only, pending CQ06 — see funnel-review.html.]

Revenue streams. Retail resale (~90% of volume) carries the business's margin at an average retail gross of ~$4,200 per car; wholesale (~10%) takes cars that don't suit retail, presumably at a lower margin (not separately confirmed). There is no recurring revenue stream, no tiered pricing and no published price.

Cost structure. The dominant cost driver is not a line item but a person: Adam's own time and judgement, applied to every lead, valuation, decision, settlement and pick-up. Variable costs are the vehicles purchased for resale; fixed costs are minimal (no marketing spend disclosed, no CRM, no dedicated premises evident beyond the home-visit model). This cost structure is precisely why the business is capacity-capped rather than budget-capped.

SWOT analysis

Strengths

  • 27+ years of trade reputation, including running Adam's own multi-brand dealership — a depth of category credibility no competitor in competitor-analysis.html can claim. Implication: this is the asset every differentiation move should be built to surface, not bury.
  • A decade-long, 10,000+ valuation radio relationship with 4CRB — a proof engine that is structurally unreplicable by any named competitor at any price. Implication: formalise this as a public marketing asset before a competitor invents a lower-credibility substitute.
  • ~25 cars a month sustained on trust alone, with no evidence of paid acquisition spend — direct proof that demand and point-of-sale conversion already work. Implication: the growth question is never "more leads," it is capacity and measurement.
  • Named, unprompted customer loyalty in testimonials (e.g. "I will only sell my cars to Adam" — Rex, Kyogle). Implication: the referral lever is real and cheap to systemise, not speculative.

Weaknesses

  • The entire conversion system — valuation logic, yes/no instinct, trust technique, payment/settlement discipline — lives only in Adam's head, undocumented (intelligence-brief-digest.html). Implication: the business is capacity-capped, unhireable-into and unsellable until this is written down.
  • Nothing about the business is measured: no lead volume, no source split, no conversion rates, no Adam-hours per deal (funnel-review.html). Implication: every growth and marketing decision from here is a guess until instrumentation exists.
  • No CRM or lead-capture system of any kind — every enquiry's fate depends on Adam personally remembering it (sales-process-review.html). Implication: this is a real operational gap for a $5M business, not a minor one.
  • A proven referral behaviour with no formal ask, no tracking and no incentive behind it. Implication: the cheapest volume lever available is currently left to chance.

Opportunities

  • No researched competitor markets to the estate or health-crisis seller — an open segment that matches Adam's existing base (market-gaps.html). Action: pilot named, sensitive messaging for this segment, drawn from Adam's own words once captured.
  • The sharpest, most repeated competitor complaint is that the price moves after inspection — a credible "one number, never moved" guarantee is open ground (differentiation-opportunities.html). Action: document valuation rules consistently enough to make the promise safely, then publish it.
  • No competitor visibly claims Northern Rivers NSW coverage. Action: name and content-market this area explicitly once service volume there is confirmed.
  • This audience increasingly finds services via AI assistants and generative search, and the current five-page brochure site is not built for that. Action: the Design-phase website rebuild (OKR4) closes this gap.

Threats

  • Key-person risk: if Adam is unavailable for any reason, the business currently stops. Response: prioritise second-buyer identification and training against a documented Adam Hall Standard.
  • Well-resourced, price-led national and multi-branch competitors (CarBuyers, We Buy Cars, carsales Instant Offer) could out-spend on paid acquisition if the business ever tries to compete on price. Response: never compete on headline price — defend the trust-first position instead (category-positioning.html).
  • Publishing a "one number, never moved" or similar guarantee before it is provably true would be more reputationally damaging than never making the claim. Response: gate every public HIGH-CONTROL-adjacent claim behind Adam's direct confirmation.
  • The brand-naming decision (CQ11 — does the brand carry Adam's personal name?) is unresolved and blocks brand and website work; starting either before it's answered risks a costly rebuild (priority-problems.html, problem 5). Response: sequence CORE answers ahead of brand/website lock-in, per recommended-next-moves.html.

Growth strategy

The applicable lever, in order, is market penetration first, market development second, product development third — deliberately in that order, because the business's constraint is not demand.

Rationale. Every diagnostic in Discover agrees: demand and point-of-sale conversion already work (~25 cars/month, no paid spend), so adding leads to an unmeasured, one-person system adds strain, not growth (success-definition.html, "explicitly not the goal"). Market penetration here means extracting more of the existing addressable demand from the existing three-market footprint by removing the capacity ceiling — not by spending more on acquisition.

  • Opportunity 1 — capacity unlock. Document the Adam Hall Standard and train a second buyer to operate to it, directly answering the constraint in intelligence-brief-digest.html. This is the single highest-leverage move available: it is the only lever that raises the ceiling rather than pushing harder against it.
  • Opportunity 2 — referral systemisation. A tested referral ask converts existing organic loyalty into a measured, repeatable acquisition channel at near-zero marginal cost — market penetration without new spend.
  • Opportunity 3 — segment and geography naming (market development). Explicitly targeting the estate/health-crisis segment and Northern Rivers coverage extends into adjacent, currently underserved ground that the business already touches informally (market-gaps.html).

Prerequisites. None of the above is safe to scale before measurement exists (Objective 1 in objectives-key-results.html) and before the valuation and settlement logic is documented enough to make public promises safely (HIGH-CONTROL). Product development — a formal e-commerce marketplace, a vehicle inspection tool, an AI "ask it anything" operating-system portal — is confirmed future or separately priced scope (phase1-work-digest.html), not this Design phase's task, and is the eventual diversification path toward the 10x/Cars4Us comparable, not a near-term lever. The staged execution of all three levers is set out in growth-roadmap.html.

Revenue model analysis

Current model. A single-offer, direct purchase-and-resale trade: buy at an individually judged price, resell retail (~90%) or wholesale (~10%), capture the spread. Strength: simple, high-trust, proven at ~$4,200 average retail gross per car across ~25 cars/month with no acquisition spend. Weakness: entirely dependent on one person's time and judgement, with no visibility into whether that gross could be higher, lower, or is already close to what the market will bear (pricing-packaging-notes.html).

Model fit. The model fits its market well: an older, trust-first audience that values certainty over the last dollar, sold against competitors whose reviews show the opposite behaviour (price moving after inspection). It does not yet fit the stated $7M and 10x ambitions, because it has no mechanism to grow beyond Adam's personal capacity.

Recommended adjustments. [Hypotheses, flagged for testing with Adam, not adopted] — document the existing retail/wholesale routing rule as a named, transparent choice rather than an invisible internal decision; test the proposed entry (free valuation) / commercial spine (retail purchase) / LTV engine (referral, repeat family transactions) offer architecture already floated in offer-worksheet.html and pricing-packaging-notes.html. No pricing or valuation-logic change should be made without Adam's direct confirmation — this is HIGH-CONTROL-adjacent territory.

New opportunities not yet explored. The premium/low-kilometre vehicle niche (Segment 4 in customer-segments.html) is visible in the existing offer but not positioned or measured as a distinct segment — potentially the highest-margin-per-transaction niche the business already touches without pursuing deliberately. A formal referral incentive is untested. The e-commerce marketplace is the longer-horizon diversification opportunity, explicitly out of this Design phase's scope.

Resource requirements

Budget ranges. [Ranges, not false precision] The commercial structure already agreed covers Discover ($5,000, credited in full), Design (quoted at Gate 1, $5,000 credited) and Deploy (monthly recurring, quoted at Gate 2) — intelligence-brief-digest.html. Beyond this, expect a real, separate cost to recruit and train a second buyer (industry-comparable hiring cost, not yet estimated), a low-cost lead-log/CRM tool (near-zero to modest monthly fee), and no new paid-media spend until the measurement baseline exists. The e-commerce marketplace, vehicle inspection tool and AI operating-system portal are explicitly excluded from this budget envelope and separately priced future scope.

Capabilities. In-house: Adam's valuation judgement (irreplaceable in the near term) and, once trained, a second buyer. Outsourced: Jewell Projects facilitates the CORE mapping and documentation; Maxxim delivers brand, website and marketing execution.

Tools & technology. Immediate: a simple lead log or call register (no new tooling required to start). Near-term: source-tagging discipline, basic time-tracking per deal. Partnerships. The existing 4CRB 89.3FM radio relationship is a strategic asset to maintain and formalise, not just a historical channel. Potential future referral relationships with allied-health, aged-care or estate-adjacent networks are untested ideas worth piloting carefully given the sensitivity involved.
