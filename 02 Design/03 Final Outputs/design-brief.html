Design Brief

1. Project overview

This brief covers the physical and digital collateral set that carries Adam Hall Buy My Car's brand direction into the real world — the pieces a designer or print supplier needs to produce, alongside the website build covered separately in website-brief.html. It exists to close a specific gap: the current business runs on a five-page WordPress brochure site, a weekly 4CRB radio segment and word of mouth, with no formalised collateral set at all beyond whatever ad-hoc materials Adam already uses (website-crawl-digest.html). This brief produces the first coherent set — business card, letterhead, email signature, social avatar, a large-print leaflet, a referral card and a partner one-pager — built to the provisional palette and type system in brand-guidelines.html.

Business objective this supports. Objective 3 in website-strategy.html — formalising the radio credibility and testimonial proof already earned into permanent, structured assets — and Bet 3 in strategic-priorities.html, which names a systemised referral ask as a currently under-invested lever. The referral card and partner one-pager exist specifically to give the referral and partnership programs in partnership-referral-plan.html something physical to hand over, since neither currently exists.

How this fits the broader plan. This is one part of a three-brief Design phase package: this document (creative direction and collateral), website-brief.html (the site build) and photo-video-brief.html (the photography and video shoot that supplies every hero image these deliverables need). All three draw on the same brand foundation — brand-strategy.html, brand-guidelines.html, logo-brief.html and copy-deck.html — and none should be actioned in isolation from the others.

What success looks like. A designer can pick up this brief and the sign-off on logo-brief.html and produce every deliverable below without a clarifying question; Adam has a consistent, warm, non-corporate physical presence wherever he meets a seller, a partner or a referral in person; and nothing produced needs to be reprinted or rebuilt if the CQ11 brand-naming decision resolves differently to the staged-transition path this brief assumes.

The single hard gate on this entire brief. No logo exists yet. logo-brief.html gates Design Studio generation of logo candidates; Adam must pick a direction and a human designer must refine it before any deliverable below goes to final production. This brief specifies every asset ready to build the moment that mark exists — it does not wait idle for it.

2. Brand context

Full detail lives in brand-strategy.html and brand-guidelines.html; the headline read a designer needs before opening either:

  • Who we are. A home-visit, direct car-buying service across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers, built around one accountable person — Adam — rather than a company, branch network or platform.
  • Archetype and personality. The Caregiver, primary; The Everyman, secondary — warm, protective and grounded. Never corporate, never a hard sell.
  • Positioning. The trust-and-ease-maximising, fully personal way to sell a car — occupying the one corner of the category no named competitor (CarBuyers, We Buy Cars, carsales Instant Offer) credibly claims. Full statement in positioning.html.
  • Tone of voice. Plain, everyday language; quietly confident, never boastful; warm and unhurried; direct enough to say "no" when a car isn't a fit. Full guide in copy-deck.html, section 6.
  • Colour, in one line. Harbour Navy (#1E4A5F) and Brass Trust (#B8863E) on a warm off-white (#FBF9F5) — deliberately apart from the category's default red/yellow/orange "sale" urgency colours. Full palette and usage rules in brand-guidelines.html.
  • Typography, in one line. Lora for headlines and any personal-voice copy; PT Sans for body copy, forms and captions — both chosen for legibility at larger sizes for a 60–75+ audience. Full type scale in brand-guidelines.html.
  • Existing identity elements to respect. None are settled yet — there is no existing logo, no confirmed print material and no locked colour or type system. Every deliverable in this brief is provisional until logo-brief.html is signed off and Brand Identity reissues brand-guidelines.html as canonical. Nothing here should be treated as a design decision that has already been made.

The CQ11 flag, carried into every deliverable below. Whether the brand permanently carries Adam's personal name is unresolved (core-questions-digest.html, CQ11). Per brand-strategy.html's recommended Scenario C, every deliverable is built to work under the current name, “Adam Hall Buy My Car,” today, while avoiding any permanent, name-locked commitment — a registered trademark, tooling with a long replacement cycle, or a print run so large it can't be reissued — until CQ11 is answered directly with Adam. This is a recommendation the brief carries forward, not a decision this brief is entitled to make final.

3. Target audience

Full personas live in customer-profile.html; this section is what a designer needs to feel, not repeat.

  • Barbara, 71 (primary) — the downsizer. Sees this collateral mostly in the moment of first contact or in a printed piece handed to her by a friend or community group. She should feel: "this looks like it's from someone real and careful, not a company trying to sell me something." Large, legible type and a calm palette matter more to her than trend-driven design.
  • Jen, 46 (secondary, highest advocacy) — the family seller. Encounters this collateral mostly through referral (a friend's recommendation, a partner one-pager at a service she's already dealing with). She should feel reassured this isn't transactional the moment she sees it — calm, dignified, never glossy or celebratory.
  • Community and partner audiences. Seniors' organisations, retirement villages, solicitors and aged-care services (partnership-referral-plan.html) encounter the partner one-pager as their first tangible sense of who Adam is professionally — it needs to read as credible and considered, not a home-made flyer.

What every piece should make someone feel. Calm, respected and unhurried — the opposite of a car-dealership flyer or a "we buy any car" sign. What action they should take. Call the number on the piece, or in the referral card's case, pass it to someone who needs it — never a QR code to a form, a discount code or a deadline device, consistent with the "no pressure" brand promise throughout copy-deck.html.

4. Key messages

Sourced from copy-deck.html; full messaging hierarchy, taglines and boilerplate live there. The headline hierarchy for collateral:

  • Primary message (use on every piece). "We come to you, tell you honestly what your car is worth, and if you want to sell, you'll have the money in your account within a day — no pressure, no strangers, no hassle." (copy-deck.html, section 2, the value proposition statement.)
  • Tagline for long-shelf-life pieces (business card, letterhead, signage, the partner one-pager). “One visit. One number. No games.” — CQ11-neutral, safe on anything with a multi-year print life.
  • Do not use on permanent or print-committed pieces: “The name you already trust, at your kitchen table” — explicitly flagged in copy-deck.html as not CQ11-neutral; near-term social and radio creative only.
  • Mandatory soft-claim rule. Never print “guaranteed,” “never changes” or any unconditional promise about the valuation. Use “a number that's meant to hold” or “Real Market Pricing, explained plainly” instead — Adam's valuation rules are not yet confirmed and consistently proven (CQ01–CQ03, HIGH-CONTROL-adjacent per positioning.html).
  • Credibility line, wherever space allows. 27+ years in the automotive trade; a decade hosting “What's Your Car Worth?” on 4CRB 89.3FM; 10,000+ live, on-air valuations since 2010 — all confirmed facts, safe to use freely (copy-deck.html, section 9).
  • Mandatory copy element on every piece. The phone number, 0404 290 617, set generously and never smaller than the surrounding body copy — matching the current site's single-CTA-per-page discipline (website-crawl-digest.html).
  • No invented testimonials or statistics. Every quote used must trace to a real, named customer already captured in copy-deck.html (Rex, Kyogle; Terry, Pottsville; Jen, Currumbin Waters) — never a new quote invented for a collateral piece.

6. Creative direction

Colour palette. Provisional, from brand-guidelines.html. Harbour Navy #1E4A5F (primary — headlines, primary CTA/phone-number treatment, vehicle signage base); Harbour Navy Deep #163846 (reversed/inverse plates, dark grounds); Brass Trust #B8863E (warmth accent, used sparingly for testimonial pull-quotes and the radio-credibility line — never as a dominant colour); Paper #FBF9F5 and Paper 2 #F2ECDF (backgrounds); Ink #23292D (body text). Full CMYK/PMS approximations and usage rules in brand-guidelines.html — none of these are confirmed swatch-book picks; confirm against a physical swatch book before any print run.

Typography guidance. Lora (serif) for headlines, pull-quotes and the wordmark treatment; PT Sans (humanist sans) for body copy, contact details and captions. No weight lighter than regular (400) anywhere — thin/light weights are explicitly against the brand's readability rule for this audience. On print collateral aimed at the 60–75+ audience specifically (the large-print leaflet, business card, referral card), err toward the larger end of any size range rather than the smaller.

Imagery style. Real, candid, people-and-place-focused — Adam himself, real customers (with consent), real driveways and homes across the three regions. Never stock photography of anonymous salespeople or showroom cars. Full imagery brief and shot list live in photo-video-brief.html; this brief's collateral should draw directly from that shoot's output rather than sourcing separate imagery.

Iconography. Deferred for V1, per brand-guidelines.html — no broader icon system is briefed here. Where a small supporting mark is needed (e.g. a phone icon beside the number), keep it a simple, flat, single-colour glyph with no automotive cliché (no steering wheels, keys or car silhouettes, per logo-brief.html's constraints).

Reference inspiration. No specific admired sites or brands were named by the client for this brief; the qualitative territory instead comes from logo-brief.html section 7 — "the calm confidence of a well-established local law or accounting firm; the restrained trustworthiness of a heritage regional bank; the warm familiarity of a community radio station." Treat these as mood references to borrow cues from (calm competence, restraint, warmth), never as brands to visually copy.

What to avoid. Bright reds, yellows and "sale" tones; chrome effects, checkered flags, speed lines or aggressive angular "fast" marks; starburst or exclamation-mark treatments; the generic "we buy any car!" van-signage look; cold, minimalist fintech-app aesthetics; any gradient, drop shadow, 3D bevel or photographic texture on the logo itself once it exists (logo-brief.html, sections 7–8).

7. Mandatory elements

  • Logo placement rules. Not yet defined — pending the refined mark from logo-brief.html. Once it exists, minimum size, clear space and approved-variant rules will be issued as an addendum to brand-guidelines.html and must be folded into every deliverable above before final print.
  • Legal disclaimers. None are currently confirmed as required beyond standard Australian Consumer Law obligations for a private vehicle-purchase transaction — confirm with Adam's own advice whether any specific disclosure (e.g. regarding PPSR checks, cooling-off, or dealer-licensing status versus a private buyer) must appear on print collateral before finalising the letterhead or referral card. Do not add legal copy speculatively.
  • Partner/co-branding requirements. The partner one-pager may need a co-branding treatment (e.g. a retirement village's own logo alongside Adam's) once a specific partner relationship is confirmed — design the layout with a clearly defined, secondary co-brand zone from the outset so this doesn't require a rebuild later. The 4CRB radio association should be credited wherever the radio credibility line appears, per logo-brief.html's "works as a co-branded... element" functional requirement, without visually competing with Adam's own mark.
  • Brand guidelines to follow. brand-guidelines.html (palette, type, imagery) and logo-brief.html (mark construction and constraints) — both explicitly provisional documents; re-check every deliverable above against the reissued, canonical versions once Brand Identity confirms the final mark and palette.

10. Approval process

  • Who reviews creative concepts. Adam Hall (client) reviews every concept directly; Maxxim's design lead reviews for brand-guideline and accessibility consistency before any concept reaches Adam.
  • Who gives final approval. Adam Hall, for every deliverable in this brief — there is no other decision-maker in this business's structure (customer-profile.html, decision-making unit logic applies equally to internal approvals).
  • How feedback is provided. Annotated PDF or a short written list of changes; a live review call is offered as an alternative for anyone more comfortable talking through changes than writing them, consistent with this client's own stated phone-first preference.
  • Turnaround time for feedback. 3–5 business days at each stage, matching the timeline in section 9. If a stage risks running long, flag it immediately rather than silently absorbing the delay into the next milestone.
