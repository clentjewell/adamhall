Website Strategy

How to read this document

This is the strategic blueprint for the rebuilt Adam Hall Buy My Car website — what it must achieve and how it converts. The full page tree lives in sitemap.html; the per-page content plans a copywriter and builder execute against live in page-strategy.html; keyword targeting and the organic-search plan live in seo-strategy.html — this document sets the SEO plumbing only and defers keywords to that sibling. All three build on brand-strategy.html, positioning.html, messaging-offer-architecture.html, customer-profile.html, customer-journey.html, messaging-by-stage.html, conversion-pathway.html, strategic-priorities.html, business-plan.html and measurement-plan.html, and on the client's live site audit in website-crawl-digest.html.

Two flags carried forward, unchanged, from every upstream document. First, whether the brand permanently carries Adam's personal name (CQ11) is unresolved (brand-strategy.html) — this strategy is written so the site works fully under the current name and does not require a structural rebuild if the brand later generalises. Second, the valuation logic and the payment-to-settlement sequence are HIGH-CONTROL, pending Adam's direct confirmation (CQ01–CQ04) — nothing below states or implies a "one number, never moved" guarantee as live; it is referenced only as a gated, future claim, per positioning.html's proof-points table.

The current site (website-crawl-digest.html) is a five-page WordPress/Elementor brochure — Home, How it Works, Buy My Car, Adam's Story, Listen, Contact — with the phone number 0404 290 617 as the only call to action, no analytics, no schema markup, and no content built for the estate/health-crisis segment or for Northern Rivers specifically. This document defines the rebuild that closes those gaps; it is not a redesign of the existing pages in place.

Website objectives

Objective 1 — Convert a stranger's first exposure into a phone call or callback, without ever pressuring the visitor

Objective. Replace the five-page brochure site with a site built to convert radio listeners, referrals, cold search visitors and AI-assistant visitors alike into a phone call or a logged callback request, never a filled-in quote form. Business goal link. Objective 4 in the Discover-phase OKRs ("build discovery and trust assets that don't depend on Adam being in the room") and Bet 4 in strategic-priorities.html. Success metric. Website-attributed leads (calls plus callback requests) tracked monthly from month 1 of launch — the "website and marketing-channel metrics" line measurement-plan.html already names as currently unstarted.

Objective 2 — Make the site legible to an older, less digitally confident visitor and to the adult child researching on a parent's behalf

Objective. Design for the audience that actually arrives — 60–75+ sellers and, separately, adult children under time and emotional pressure — without asking either to do more than confirm a phone number. Business goal link. The Barbara and Jen personas in customer-profile.html; market gap 1 (sellers who won't use an online form at all) in the Discover-phase market-gaps work. Success metric. WCAG 2.1 AA conformance verified at launch; a "find the number and call" and "submit a callback request" task tested with an older test-user cohort before launch, not assumed to work from design intent alone.

Objective 3 — Formalise the radio credibility and testimonial proof already earned into permanent, structured on-site assets

Objective. Turn the 10,000+ live on-air valuations since 2010 and the five named testimonials from background facts into a public, verifiable proof asset a cold visitor can check without ever hearing the radio segment. Business goal link. Bet 4 in strategic-priorities.html ("claim the uncontested market position"). Success metric. A named, quotable radio-credibility line live on every priority page (page-strategy.html); a repeatable testimonial/review-collection habit in place by month 3, growing the count beyond today's five.

Objective 4 — Give the estate/health-crisis segment and Northern Rivers a dedicated home neither has today

Objective. Build a page for the adult child selling a parent's or partner's car, and a page that claims Northern Rivers coverage explicitly — both open ground no researched competitor occupies. Business goal link. Bet 4 in strategic-priorities.html; market gaps 3 and 4 (estate/health-crisis sellers; Northern Rivers coverage). Success metric. The family-car-sale page and the Northern Rivers regional page live at launch; enquiries tagged to either in the lead log, tracked from month 1.

Objective 5 — Instrument the site from day one

Objective. Stop the website being the only completely unmeasured part of the business. Business goal link. Objective 1 in the Discover-phase OKRs ("make the business's engine visible") and measurement-plan.html's explicit statement that "no analytics exist on the current five-page brochure site." Success metric. Analytics and call tracking live at launch; the first monthly website report produced by month 2, feeding the same monthly report the rest of the business already reports against.

Information architecture rationale

The full page tree lives in sitemap.html; this section is the reasoning behind it.

Two visitor mental models arrive at the same site. A majority already recognise Adam's name from radio or word of mouth and want the phone number fast, with minimal reading — for them, the site's job is confirmation, not persuasion. A minority — disproportionately Jen's segment, researching "how do I sell my father's car" cold via search or an AI assistant — arrive with zero context and need enough proof and specificity before they'll call at all. The architecture serves "I know who this is, give me the number" from anywhere in one click, while giving the cold, research-mode visitor a page that speaks to her exact situation rather than a generic homepage she has to self-translate.

Business priorities shape what sits near the top. Per strategic-priorities.html Bet 4, the highest-priority unclaimed proof asset is named-person continuity plus the radio-and-testimonial engine, so About/Adam's Story and Testimonials sit as full primary-navigation pages, not buried sub-pages. The estate/health-crisis track and the three regional pages are new, full peer-level pages rather than footnotes, because a visitor arriving via a specific search needs to land on content built for her situation, not a page she has to reinterpret.

Why the hierarchy is flat. This is a phone-first, single-core-service business — there is no product catalogue to nest, so a deep hierarchy would only add clicks between a visitor and the phone number. Every page sits at most one level from the home page; the three regional pages nest one level under the Sell Your Car hub only because they share, and must not repeat, its core offer explanation.

What's deferred. The e-commerce marketplace — confirmed separate, later-priced scope per business-plan.html — has zero presence in this tree; it is a different product line for a later stage, not a hidden section of this site. A public blog/resource centre is deferred until a repeatable content-production habit exists, since no editorial resource is confirmed yet (audience-teardown.html); the FAQ page absorbs the near-term content need instead. The full deliberately-not-built list, with reasons, is in sitemap.html.

User flows

Flow 1 — Barbara, the downsizer, primed by radio

Entry point. Word of mouth or a half-remembered radio segment; she searches "Adam Hall buy my car" or types a URL she was given. Key pages. Home → a quick scan of the testimonial strip for reassurance → How It Works → calls directly from the persistent phone number, without necessarily reaching a dedicated contact page. Conversion point. A phone call placed from the sticky header or footer number. Critical path. The home page hero and the trust signal immediately beneath it (27 years, 4CRB) must confirm "yes, this is the fellow from the radio" within the first screen, so she calls without needing to read deeply — matching Stage 1 (Exposure) in customer-journey.html.

Flow 2 — Jen, the family seller, cold search or AI assistant

Entry point. A Google or AI-assistant search for "sell my father's car" or "how to sell a parent's car Gold Coast," since she typically has no prior radio relationship. Key pages. Search/AI result → the Selling a Parent's or Family Member's Car page → Testimonials, specifically the Currumbin Waters story that mirrors her situation → FAQ, answering "does my father need to be present" → Contact / Request a Callback. Conversion point. The callback-request form is weighted as the primary conversion for this flow, since she is often managing several things at once and may not be ready to talk the moment she lands; a phone call remains equally available. Critical path. The family-car-sale page must speak to her exact situation in its first two sentences — not "we buy cars" but the same-care, no-transactional-feel promise — or she bounces to a more generic competitor page.

Flow 3 — Graham, the licence-loss seller, compressed and opportunistic

Entry point. Word of mouth or radio, arriving under an externally forced, fairly short timeline. Key pages. Home → How It Works, scanned quickly for "how long does this actually take" → Contact. No dedicated page is built for this segment, consistent with the targeting call in customer-profile.html — his flow relies on the same primary pages doing enough reassurance work fast. Conversion point. A phone call, same mechanism as Barbara's flow. Critical path. How It Works must state the timeline explicitly and briefly (days, not weeks), since speed is his top decision criterion.

Content requirements

  • Home hero. Exists today but must be rebuilt — the current value stack is generic ("personal, simple, fast, easy, hassle-free"); the new hero must lead with named-person trust and the phone CTA in the first screen.
  • Core service description (the home-visit valuation and direct purchase). Exists in today's "How it Works" copy — needs rewriting for plain language and larger type, not new invention.
  • Estate/health-crisis track content. Must be created — a confirmed open gap (market gap 3) with no equivalent on the current site.
  • Regional content (Gold Coast, Brisbane, Northern Rivers). Must be created, each with genuinely unique local copy and proof — never a doorway-page stamp-out with a swapped place name.
  • Radio/credibility content hub. Exists informally as a "Listen" page today — must be rebuilt as a proper proof and content asset, not just a link out to the station.
  • Testimonials. Exist as scattered site copy (five named quotes) — must be reformatted into one dedicated, structured, situation-organised asset.
  • FAQ. Exists only implicitly in current page copy — must be made explicit and expanded, covering the three confirmed objection patterns plus estate/health-crisis-specific questions.
  • Case studies (longer-form). Not built at this stage; testimonials serve this role until more proof exists — a fuller case-study format is future scope, not this rebuild's task.
  • Blog/resource categories. Do not exist and are not built now — see the Information Architecture rationale above for why.

Conversion strategy

Primary conversion point. The phone call. Every page carries a persistent, prominent tel: link — in the header on desktop, and a sticky footer button on mobile — matching this audience's stated preference for a phone call over any form or app (customer-profile.html, messaging-by-stage.html). This is not a new mechanism; the rebuild formalises and prioritises what already works.

Secondary conversion point. A callback-request form for visitors not ready, or not free, to call immediately: name, phone number, suburb, best time to call, and an optional free-text "tell us about your car" — five fields, no more. There is deliberately no online valuation tool, rego lookup or multi-step wizard: a valuation is a home-visit judgement, not an algorithm, and pretending otherwise would contradict the brand's explicit "no games" tone (messaging-offer-architecture.html). Conversion on this site is a phone call or a callback request — never a web form standing in for the valuation itself.

Lead capture approach. Single-step form, five fields maximum, no account creation, no CAPTCHA (an accessibility risk for this audience), light validation only.

Trust-signal placement. A testimonial or the radio-credibility line sits directly beside every CTA, not only gathered on a separate testimonials page. Per conversion-pathway.html's "offer and incentive at each conversion point," the incentive to act now is the existing free, obligation-free offer, made explicit — never a discount or deadline device, which would contradict the "no pressure" brand promise.

Exit/abandonment recovery. None built at launch. No paid retargeting exists today, and none is proposed until the measurement baseline exists (strategic-priorities.html is explicit that no demand-side spend should precede instrumentation). The only "recovery" mechanism is the callback-request field itself, capturing visitors not ready to call immediately so they aren't lost the way a purely brochure-style site loses them today.

Technical requirements

CMS recommendation. A static site, not a rebuild of the current WordPress/Elementor site in place. Rationale: the current site has no analytics, no structured schema, no per-suburb pages and no AI-visibility structuring (website-crawl-digest.html) — patching WordPress to add all of this is more effort than a rebuild that ships these by default, and a static output directly serves the Core Web Vitals and accessibility targets below.

Integrations. Call tracking, so phone conversions are attributable to source — closing the single biggest measurement gap named in the Discover-phase funnel review; web analytics; Google Search Console; Google Business Profile, verified and aligned to one NAP source. No CRM integration is proposed beyond the manual lead log already recommended in measurement-plan.html — a CRM is a later Deploy-phase decision once volume from the site is proven.

Performance targets. Core Web Vitals green (LCP, INP, CLS) — the realistic target on a static rebuild with optimised images and no render-blocking scripts.

Accessibility target. WCAG 2.1 AA as the floor, with deliberate choices beyond AA for this specific audience: minimum 18px body text, strong colour contrast, large tap targets (48px minimum) for the phone and callback CTAs, no auto-advancing carousels, no CAPTCHA, single-step forms, and no pattern (accordion-only content, hover-triggered menus) that assumes a confident, mouse-fluent visitor.

Mobile-first considerations. The sticky call button is a mobile-first pattern, not a desktop feature ported down — most enquiries are expected to arrive by phone in hand, consistent with the established phone-first channel preference.

Phase recommendation. Build now. The current site is named as a strategic gap in website-crawl-digest.html ("not a scalable brand asset... no visible SEO/AI-search structuring") and sits directly under Discover-phase Objective 4, which does not depend on Adam's CORE answers or the CQ11 naming decision — it can proceed in parallel with the documentation work in strategic-priorities.html Bet 1, provided the site is built to work fully under the current name and would not need a structural rebuild if CQ11 later resolves differently.

SEO foundation

This is the site's SEO plumbing; keywords and priorities are taken from seo-strategy.html rather than re-derived here.

URL structure. Flat, human-readable slugs — for example /sell-your-car, /sell-your-car/gold-coast, /sell-your-car/brisbane, /sell-your-car/northern-rivers, /family-car-sale, /whats-your-car-worth, /about, /testimonials, /faq, /contact. No query strings, no deep nesting.

Title/meta approach. A consistent pattern per page type — primary local or intent keyword, then a short service descriptor, then the brand name. The full formula and worked examples live in seo-strategy.html.

Schema markup. LocalBusiness JSON-LD site-wide (name, address, phone, hours, service area, services) as the single highest-impact fix; Service schema on the Sell Your Car hub and the three regional pages; FAQPage schema on the FAQ page; Review schema deferred until a genuine public review platform exists — not fabricated ahead of that.

Internal linking strategy. Every regional and segment page links back to Testimonials, FAQ and Contact; the home page links to all three regional pages and the family-car-sale page directly from primary navigation, not buried in a footer — deliberate, given how much of the estate/health-crisis and Northern Rivers opportunity depends on being findable rather than merely existing.

Analytics & measurement

Key events to track. Phone-number clicks (tel: link taps), callback-form submissions, FAQ engagement (which objections get read most), and regional-page visits by page — to see whether Northern Rivers traffic actually materialises rather than assuming it will.

Conversion funnels. (1) Landing page → phone click. (2) Landing page → callback-form submit. (3) Regional or segment page → either conversion type, to judge whether the dedicated pages are pulling their weight.

Tools required. Web analytics, call tracking that attributes phone conversions to the page and source that generated them, and Google Search Console for organic visibility.

Reporting cadence. Monthly, feeding directly into the same monthly business report already defined in measurement-plan.html — website metrics are a new line in that report, not a separate one running in isolation.
