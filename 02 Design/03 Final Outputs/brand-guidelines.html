Brand Guidelines

How to use this book — and its provisional status

Everything in the Colour, Typography and Logos sections of this book is provisional. No logo assets exist yet for this client — the Design Studio has not generated or received a mark, and Brand Identity has not run. This document is written against two grounded sources instead: the visual expression already live on the current site (website-crawl-digest.html) and the “Visual Identity Direction” strategic brief in brand-strategy.html. It sets a confident, executable starting direction so downstream work (the Logo Brief and, later, the website) has something real to build from — but none of the hex values, fonts or logo treatment below should be treated as final, printed, or trademarked until logo-brief.html is signed off by Adam and the Design Studio has generated and refined an actual mark.

This brand book exists to keep every downstream decision consistent while that work happens. It follows the client’s own framing of this Design phase as a “brand book revamp” and a “tickle-up, not a rebuild” of branding already considered sound — so the direction below sharpens and formalises the current visual expression rather than replacing it. Read it alongside brand-strategy.html (the platform this book documents), logo-brief.html (the creative brief the Studio will generate against) and, once written, copy-deck.html (the words that go with this look).

Once Adam signs off the Logo Brief and the Design Studio produces and a human designer refines an actual mark, this document should be re-issued with the settled hex/CMYK/PMS, fonts and logo files it currently only proposes. Until then, treat every value below as a direction, not a decision.

Strategy foundation

This book documents — it does not re-decide — the platform settled in brand-strategy.html. Full detail lives there; the headline read:

  • Who we are. A home-visit, direct car-buying service for the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers, built around one accountable person rather than a company, branch network or platform.
  • Who we serve. Car sellers aged 60–75 and older — downsizers, licence-loss sellers, and estate or health-driven family sellers — who want relief and trust, not a negotiation. [Segment ages and boundaries are hypothesis, not yet volume-tested with Adam.]
  • Archetype. The Caregiver, primary; The Everyman, secondary — warm, protective and grounded, never corporate or blunt.
  • Values. Empathy first, then the solution · honesty with reasons · one number that doesn’t move · stay in the room · ownership, not excuses (“the Adam Hall Standard”).
  • Positioning. The trust-and-ease-maximising, fully personal way to sell a car — the one corner of the category no competitor occupies credibly. Full statement in positioning.html.
  • Essence. A known, accountable person, at your home, with a number that’s meant to hold.
  • Tagline direction. Recommended for long-shelf-life assets: “One visit. One number. No games.” or “We come to you. We don’t move the goalposts.” — both CQ11-neutral. Full tagline set and rationale in brand-strategy.html and copy-deck.html.

The open decision this book carries forward, unresolved: whether the brand permanently carries Adam’s personal name (CQ11). Per brand-strategy.html, the recommended path is Scenario C — keep “Adam Hall Buy My Car” as the customer-facing brand now, while deliberately building “The Adam Hall Standard” as a named, documented sub-brand underneath it. Every visual decision in this book is made so it survives that staged path: nothing here should need to be thrown away if Adam later confirms Scenario B, and nothing here forces the decision early. See logo-brief.html for how this plays out in the mark itself.

Logos

No logo assets exist for this client yet. The current site (website-crawl-digest.html) is a WordPress/Elementor brochure site; the crawl captured no distinct logo mark or favicon treatment beyond the wordmark “Adam Hall” used as a page title. There is nothing published on the CDN to reference here, and nothing to reverse-engineer as a settled mark.

This section is deliberately a requirements brief, not a logo. The full creative direction — concept territories, form language, colour and typography direction, and Studio starting prompts — is written out in full in logo-brief.html, which gates logo generation in the Design Studio. Once the Studio has produced candidates, Adam has picked one, and a human designer has refined it into the full variant set, this section will be rewritten with the real thing: the primary mark, mono and inverse variants, a stand-alone brandmark/icon, clear-space and minimum-size rules, and background/inverse usage rules, each with a published CDN image.

Two requirements are already locked in by the strategy, and should constrain whatever the Studio produces:

  • CQ11-aware construction. Until the brand-naming decision is made, the mark should be built as a combination mark (a distinct symbol alongside a wordmark), not a purely typographic lockup of “Adam Hall” with no independent symbol. A strong symbol element can carry forward and eventually stand alone for “The Adam Hall Standard” if Scenario B or C's later stage is chosen — a wordmark-only logo cannot do this without a full rebuild.
  • Functional range. Must read clearly at favicon/app-icon and social-avatar size, work in a single flat colour, and reverse cleanly onto the primary dark colour (Harbour Navy, see Colour below) — the mark needs to work equally on a phone screen, a printed flyer and vehicle signage.

Iconography and a broader graphic-element system are deferred for V1 — noted here as future work, not built out with placeholders.

Colour

Provisional palette, grounded in the strategy, pending Logo Brief sign-off and Design Studio generation. The direction below deliberately continues what already works on the current site rather than inventing a new look: the site today reads calm and trustworthy rather than “sale”-driven, with no pricing shown anywhere and warm, personal copy (website-crawl-digest.html). The palette below sharpens that into a real system and is built specifically to sit apart from the category’s default red/yellow/“sale” urgency colours (brand-strategy.html Visual Identity Direction, citing CarBuyers and We Buy Cars per competitor-analysis.html) — the territory is closer to a respected local tradesperson or financial adviser than a car dealership.

Typography

Provisional, pending Logo Brief sign-off. The direction follows brand-strategy.html exactly: warm and highly legible above all else, because this audience skews 60–75+, and readability at larger sizes on a phone screen or a printed flyer matters more than trend-driven type choices. The personality to convey is “a letter from someone you trust”, not a tech start-up or a finance app.

Primary (headline) font — Lora. A warm, well-proportioned serif with generous curves and a calm, personal feel — closer to a handwritten letter than a corporate report. Used for headlines, pull-quotes and the wordmark treatment in the logo lockup.

Secondary (body/UI) font — PT Sans. A plain, highly legible humanist sans-serif designed for readability, with a generous x-height that holds up well at larger sizes and on small screens. Used for body copy, navigation, forms, buttons and captions — anywhere clarity matters more than character.

Readability rule for this audience: body copy should never run smaller than 17–18px on web, with a line-height of at least 1.6 and no font weight lighter than regular (400). Avoid thin/light weights entirely — they are harder to read for older eyes and undercut the trustworthy, grounded feel this brand needs.

Imagery direction

Carried directly from brand-strategy.html: real, candid, people-and-place-focused. Adam himself, real customers, real driveways and homes across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers — never stock photography of anonymous salespeople or showroom cars.

  • Real over staged. Genuine home-visit moments, a handshake at the door, a driveway conversation — not a studio backdrop or a stock “happy customer with keys” shot.
  • Warmth and quiet dignity, never gloss. Given the weight of the estate/health-crisis segment (customer-profile.html, customer-pain-points.html), imagery should never look like a hard sell or a celebratory car-dealership moment — calm and respectful over polished and promotional.
  • People over product. Adam and the customer are the subject; the car is present but secondary — this is a brand about a relationship, not a vehicle.
  • Place matters. Recognisable Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers settings (driveways, suburban streets, regional towns) reinforce the “local, known person” position rather than an anonymous national brand.

Keyword set for briefing photography or selecting stock as a stopgap: warm, candid, unhurried, local, honest, dignified, personal — never glossy, corporate, showroom, urgent, salesy.

In Practice

Applications are described here as requirements for whoever builds them next (the website build, a printer, a signwriter) — not as finished mockups, since no logo exists yet to place into them. Once the Logo Brief is signed off and a mark exists, this section should be revisited with real application examples.

  • Business card. Harbour Navy or Paper background, logo lockup (once it exists) with Lora-set name and PT Sans-set contact details; phone number sized generously, consistent with the site’s current “phone number as the primary CTA on every page” pattern (website-crawl-digest.html).
  • Letterhead. Paper background, logo top-left or centred, Lora for any personal salutation copy, PT Sans for body — reinforcing the “a letter from someone you trust” feel.
  • Email signature. Compact horizontal logo lockup, PT Sans for name/title/phone, Harbour Navy for the phone number as the clear call-to-action, matching the site’s single-CTA-per-page discipline.
  • Social avatar. Icon-only mark (see logo-brief.html, lockup requirements) on a Harbour Navy or Paper ground — must survive a circular crop and read clearly at small size.
  • Website. Paper as the primary background, Paper 2 for alternating sections, Harbour Navy for headlines and primary CTAs, Brass Trust used sparingly for testimonial and credibility moments (the “as heard on 4CRB” proof asset, per differentiation-opportunities.html); body set in PT Sans at the larger, 60–75+-appropriate sizes specified above.
  • Signage (vehicle and print). Logo built to read at a distance in a single flat colour on Harbour Navy or white; no gradients, no photographic effects — per the functional requirements in logo-brief.html.
