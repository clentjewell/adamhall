Photo/Video Brief

1. Project overview

What's being produced and why. This brief scopes the single biggest asset gap in the whole Design phase: authentic imagery and video of Adam actually doing the work. No such material exists today — the current site (website-crawl-digest.html) carries no distinct photography of Adam at a home visit, and every downstream document (brand-guidelines.html, website-brief.html, design-brief.html) specifies "real, candid, Adam-at-work" imagery it cannot yet source. This shoot exists to close that gap once, with a repurposing plan that feeds every channel rather than shooting narrowly for one use.

Business objective this supports. Objective 3 in website-strategy.html — formalising the radio and testimonial proof into permanent, structured assets — and the imagery direction already committed to in brand-strategy.html and brand-guidelines.html: "real over staged... people over product... never stock photography of anonymous salespeople or showroom cars." Every other Design-phase deliverable (the website's hero images, the collateral in design-brief.html, the social content in social-strategy.html) is written assuming this shoot's output exists; none of it should fall back to stock imagery as a permanent solution.

Channels this content serves. The rebuilt website (hero and section imagery per website-brief.html); the print and digital collateral set in design-brief.html (business card, letterhead, large-print leaflet, referral card, partner one-pager); Facebook, the confirmed primary social platform (social-strategy.html); and a YouTube video library supporting AI-chatbot and generative-search visibility for the new site (social-strategy.html, Objective 4).

What this brief is not. It does not brief stock photography as an acceptable substitute at any point — stock imagery of anonymous salespeople, showroom cars or staged "happy customer with keys" moments is explicitly the wrong direction for this brand (brand-guidelines.html, Imagery direction). Where a genuine gap exists before this shoot can happen, the honest fallback is to under-populate a section with fewer, real images rather than fill it with stock.

2. Creative direction

Overall visual style and mood. Warm, candid, unhurried, local, honest, dignified, personal — the exact keyword set specified in brand-guidelines.html. Never glossy, corporate, showroom, urgent or salesy. The reference emotional register throughout is "a letter from someone you trust," not a commercial car-buying advertisement.

Colour palette direction. Nothing in the shoot itself should be colour-graded or styled to specific brand hex values — that would look artificial for real photography — but wardrobe, prop and location choices should avoid clashing with the brand palette (Harbour Navy #1E4A5F, Brass Trust #B8863E, warm off-white tones) and should specifically avoid saturated reds, yellows or "sale"-adjacent colours in clothing or signage that appears in frame, consistent with brand-guidelines.html's "what to avoid" note.

Lighting direction. Natural light throughout, wherever possible — overcast or soft morning/late-afternoon daylight for exterior driveway and doorstep shots; window light for interior moments. No harsh studio lighting, no on-camera flash that would flatten the warmth of a real home setting. For the radio-studio shots, work with the station's existing practical lighting rather than bringing in a full studio rig — the goal is a genuine behind-the-scenes feel, not a polished commercial set.

Reference images or videos. No specific photographer, director or campaign was named by the client as a style reference. The qualitative territory instead comes directly from brand-guidelines.html and logo-brief.html: think of the restrained, dignified photography style of a respected local financial adviser's or family law firm's "meet the team" page, or the warm, unposed documentary style used by community radio stations to profile a long-running local segment — never a car-dealership or classifieds-site photography style.

What to avoid. Studio backdrops; stock "happy customer with keys" poses; showroom or forecourt settings; staged handshake-over-a-desk photography; anything that looks like a car-dealership advertisement; forced or performative smiling; any imagery that could read as celebratory or triumphant in the estate/health-crisis context (see section 2's sensitivity note below and the full sensitivity guidance in section 5).

A sensitivity note that governs the whole shoot. A meaningful share of this brand's imagery need touches the estate/health-crisis segment — sellers acting on behalf of an unwell or deceased family member (customer-profile.html, Jen persona; content-strategy.html, Pillar 4). No shot in this brief should stage grief, ill health or a family loss for the camera. Where this segment's imagery need is genuinely unmet by what real, consented customers can offer, the honest response is fewer images, warmly and respectfully composed, never a staged re-enactment of a hard moment.

Model release and consent requirements — the process, not invented specifics. Every real customer, family member or station staff member appearing in any photograph or video must sign a written model release before the material is used in any customer-facing channel, covering at minimum: what the image/footage will be used for (website, social, print collateral), how long the consent lasts, and whether the release can be withdrawn later. This brief does not specify a particular release form, retention period or withdrawal mechanism — those are legal and operational details for Adam and Maxxim to confirm together (and, where useful, with Adam's own legal advice) before the shoot, not assumptions this brief is entitled to make. For the estate/health-crisis segment specifically, consent must be sought with particular care and patience, and no one should ever feel pressured to participate in exchange for, or in proximity to, the sale of their car — the two must be kept entirely separate in how the ask is made.

Radio audio/testimonial consent — a second, separate flag. Using any 4CRB broadcast material (V03) requires the station's own consent and rights confirmation, separate from any individual customer's release — per CQ19, this has not yet been established. Using any existing customer testimonial audio (if such a recording exists beyond the written quotes in copy-deck.html) similarly requires that specific individual's consent for this new use, even if they originally gave a quote for a different purpose. Do not assume a written testimonial quote already published implies consent for a new video or audio use.

7. Props & styling

Products to feature. A real customer's own vehicle, unstyled and undetailed beyond what it would genuinely look like on the day of a real home visit — no professional car-detailing or staging that would misrepresent the "honest, unglamorised" direction.

Equipment or tools in shots. Whatever Adam genuinely carries to a real valuation — a phone or tablet for paperwork, a folder, keys. No invented prop equipment that isn't part of his actual process.

Set dressing. None beyond what already exists at the real locations — this is documentary-style photography of real settings, not a styled set. Avoid tidying a location to the point it no longer looks like a genuinely lived-in home.

Wardrobe details. Covered in section 5; the only additional note is to avoid any branded clothing item (a competitor's logo, an unrelated business's uniform) appearing in frame by accident.

Hair and makeup direction. None — no professional hair or makeup styling for Adam or any customer. This is a "real person, real moment" shoot; any visible styling effort would contradict the entire creative direction in section 2.

8. Technical specifications

Photography

  • Camera format and resolution. Full-frame or equivalent digital camera, minimum 24-megapixel capture, to allow cropping flexibility across the aspect ratios below without quality loss.
  • File format. RAW capture, delivered as processed high-quality JPG or TIFF for use, with RAW files archived.
  • Colour profile. sRGB for all web and social use; Adobe RGB retained in the RAW/archive files in case a future print use (per design-brief.html's collateral set) needs the wider gamut.
  • Aspect ratios needed. 16:9 (website hero, YouTube thumbnails); 4:5 (Facebook feed); 1:1 (social avatar backup, Facebook square posts); 9:16 not required for this business given no confirmed vertical-video platform use (Instagram and TikTok are explicitly skipped per social-strategy.html).

Video

  • Resolution. 4K capture (delivered/published at 1080p to balance quality and file size for web embedding).
  • Frame rate. 24 or 25fps for a natural, unhurried feel consistent with the brand's tone — not 60fps, which reads as slick/commercial rather than warm and real.
  • Codec and delivery format. H.264 MP4 for web/YouTube delivery; original camera-native files archived.
  • Aspect ratios per platform. 16:9 primary (website, YouTube); a 1:1 or 4:5 cutdown for Facebook, per the format guidance in social-strategy.html.
  • Audio specifications. Lavalier or shotgun microphone for all dialogue (never rely on camera-body audio alone, given the outdoor driveway settings); 48kHz/16-bit minimum for delivered audio.

Cropping guidance. When cropping any hero image or frame to a tighter format, always preserve Adam and the customer as the compositional subject — per brand-guidelines.html's "people over product" direction, never crop down to a tight product/car-only shot as a substitute, even where a wider crop would be visually simpler.

Text overlay considerations. Where a secondary format (a Facebook square post, a print flyer image panel) needs a text overlay, use PT Sans per brand-guidelines.html's typography direction, at a size that remains legible at the smallest platform display size, and leave clear, uncluttered negative space in the original composition specifically for this — do not add heavy text over a busy part of the frame after the fact.

10. Production logistics

Shoot schedule (half-day breakdown, illustrative — confirm exact timing with Adam and each location once dates are set).

  • Day 1, morning: Home-visit-style photography and video at a real or representative driveway location (P01–P06, P08, V01, V02) — scheduled for soft early-morning light.
  • Day 1, afternoon: Portrait and detail work (P05, P10) at the same or a nearby location, using softer late-afternoon light.
  • Day 2: 4CRB studio visit (P09, and V03 only once cleared per the flag in section 4), scheduled around the station's live broadcast timing.
  • Day 3 (or a later, separately scheduled date): Any additional regional location (a second or third driveway setting for Brisbane/Northern Rivers variety) and any testimonial video sessions (V04), scheduled once willing, consenting customers are confirmed — do not force this into Day 1 or 2 if consent and scheduling aren't ready.

Pre-production checklist. Real or representative customer talent confirmed and model releases prepared (not yet signed — signed on the day, per section 5); locations scouted and homeowner permission confirmed; 4CRB studio access and timing confirmed directly with the station, separate from the CQ19 rights question; equipment (camera, lav/shotgun mic, any reflector/natural-light modifier) hired or confirmed available; weather contingency dates held for both driveway locations; a shot-by-shot checklist derived from sections 3 and 4 printed or shared with the crew ahead of the day.

Post-production requirements. Photography: light, natural retouching only — skin/blemish smoothing kept minimal and never enough to look airbrushed; colour correction for consistency across locations and light conditions, not a stylised grade. Video: a warm, natural colour grade matching the photography's treatment; editing approach should preserve natural pauses and unhurried pacing rather than fast-cutting for energy; captions/subtitles burned in or provided as a separate file for every video, both for accessibility and for sound-off social viewing. Motion graphics: minimal — a simple lower-third caption (name, situation) for testimonial subjects is sufficient; no heavy branded motion-graphics package is required or recommended, consistent with the brand's understated tone.

Delivery format. File naming: adamhall_[shotID or videoID]_[description]_[YYYYMMDD] — for example adamhall_P01_driveway-arrival_20260915. Folder structure: separated by shoot day and then by asset type (photography RAW, photography delivered, video raw, video delivered). Delivery method: uploaded via Maxxim's asset-upload skill to the client's asset CDN (assets.maxxim.ai), so every image and video referenced in website-brief.html and design-brief.html resolves to a stable, permanent URL rather than a locally held file. Delivery deadline: to be confirmed once shoot dates are locked, allowing realistic post-production time before the website build in website-brief.html needs final imagery.
