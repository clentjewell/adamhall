Customer Journey

How to read this document

This is the end-to-end journey for the two priority segments named in customer-profile.html — downsizers (Barbara) and estate/health-driven family sellers (Jen) — from first exposure to advocacy. It builds on the step-by-step process already documented in sales-process-review.html and the stage map in funnel-review.html, but reframes both around the customer's headspace rather than Adam's workflow. Licence-loss sellers (Graham) move through a compressed version of the same stages, noted where the experience meaningfully differs; the persona is not a dedicated journey in this document, consistent with the targeting call in customer-profile.html. The words for each stage — what we actually say — live in messaging-by-stage.html, not here.

A structural note that shapes every stage below: today, every stage of this journey is run by one person. That single fact, already established as the most important finding in sales-process-review.html, is also the biggest journey risk — nothing downstream of "Adam personally answers the phone" is designed to work without him, which means the journey as it exists today has no real redundancy at its most human moments.

Stage 1 — Exposure

Thinks / Feels / Does. Barbara isn't looking for anything yet — she is half-listening to the radio in the kitchen, or hearing a neighbour mention "that fellow who buys cars." There is no active search happening; exposure at this stage plants a name, not a call to action. She feels mild, background awareness rather than urgency. Jen, by contrast, is not yet exposed at all in most cases — exposure for the estate/health segment typically has not happened before the triggering event, which is why referral matters so much more for her once the moment arrives.

Touchpoints we control. The 4CRB "What's Your Car Worth?" radio segment; the website as a brochure-style presence; whatever social or community presence exists in target suburbs; word-of-mouth is influenced by the experience we design in later stages, even though we don't fully control when or how it's repeated.

Moments of truth. Whether Adam is heard as a specific, named, trustworthy individual — or as a generic "we buy cars" voice indistinguishable from the category's low-trust reputation — is decided here, before any direct contact happens. Get this wrong and the audience never differentiates Adam from an instant-offer aggregator; get it right and the later stages start from a position of pre-existing trust rather than having to build it from zero.

Where the journey breaks today. There is no measured lead-source data at all (confirmed gap, pending CQ06 in funnel-review.html), so it isn't known how much of this stage is actually working versus assumed to be working on hypothesis. The website exists but is not designed as a lead-generation engine, meaning anyone exposed digitally — rather than through radio or word of mouth — currently lands on a brochure, not a path forward.

Stage 2 — Consideration

Thinks / Feels / Does. The nagging task has become slightly less avoidable — a registration renewal, a family conversation, a health event. Barbara starts weighing whether this is finally the moment, comparing (loosely, not systematically) the effort of a private sale against just calling the name she already half-recognises. Jen, once the trigger has hit, moves through this stage fast — she is not weighing options for long, she is looking for the fastest credible path that won't feel careless.

Touchpoints we control. Testimonials and FAQ content on the website, answering the three likely objections (is it free, is there an obligation, will my car be wanted) before they're asked aloud; any referral conversation happening informally between past customers and prospective ones, which we influence through experience quality but don't script.

Moments of truth. The FAQ and testimonial content doing the pre-selling work of answering "is this actually free and obligation-free" before the phone call happens — if this doubt survives into the call itself, the call starts on the back foot instead of confirming what the person already half-believed.

Where the journey breaks today. There is no nurture mechanism at all between exposure and contact (confirmed in funnel-review.html) — someone who considers but isn't ready today has no path back to Adam other than remembering him later unprompted. For Jen specifically, there is no content addressing the estate/health situation directly; the site speaks to the transaction, not to the relief of delegation during a hard season, a gap already flagged as an opportunity in jobs-to-be-done.html.

Stage 3 — First contact

Thinks / Feels / Does. Picks up the phone — the one channel this audience trusts over a form or an app. Barbara feels a small flutter of "am I doing the right thing calling," which resolves quickly once she's talking to a real person rather than a script. Jen is direct and practical on this call, wanting to establish quickly that this won't feel transactional given everything else she's managing.

Touchpoints we control. The phone call itself, currently answered personally by Adam every time; whatever is said in the first sixty seconds, which either confirms or contradicts the trust built in stages 1 and 2.

Moments of truth. This is the single highest-leverage human moment in the whole journey after the home visit itself — the first voice on the phone either sounds like the same trustworthy, unhurried person from the radio, or it doesn't. This is a moment that must stay human; it is not a candidate for automation or intake scripting that reads as call-centre.

Where the journey breaks today. There is no lead-capture record of any kind (confirmed in sales-process-review.html) — every enquiry's fate depends entirely on Adam remembering it personally. There is also no qualification checklist or documented judgement for sorting a genuine seller from a time-waster, which means this stage cannot currently be run by anyone other than Adam, and cannot be measured (how many calls come in, how many convert to a booked visit) at all.

Stage 4 — Home visit and decision

Thinks / Feels / Does. Barbara lets a stranger into her home for the first time in this process — the moment the trust built by radio and reputation is actually tested in person. She listens to the valuation explanation, decides whether the number feels fair, and says yes or no with (per the offer) genuinely no obligation either way. Jen, if present, is watching for whether her father is treated with dignity, not just whether the number is right.

Touchpoints we control. The visit itself — who attends, how the valuation is explained, the tone and pace of the conversation, and how clearly "no obligation" is demonstrated rather than just stated.

Moments of truth. This is the moment of truth for the entire business model — the in-home visit is the trust moment, full stop. Every earlier stage exists to get the seller comfortable enough to reach this point; every later stage depends on this one going well. This must stay a human, in-person moment; it is not a candidate for remote valuation, chatbot triage, or any substitute that removes the person from the room.

Where the journey breaks today. The valuation logic itself lives entirely in Adam's judgement, undocumented (confirmed HIGH-CONTROL gap in sales-process-review.html, pending CQ01–CQ03) — this is a capacity and continuity risk, not a trust risk, since the evidence says the visit itself works. The retail-versus-wholesale routing decision is also an undocumented gut call (pending CQ03), invisible to the seller but material to the business's margin.

Stage 5 — Settlement and pick-up

Thinks / Feels / Does. Having said yes, Barbara wants certainty the paperwork is right and the money will actually arrive — this is where lingering doubt about "is this too good to be true" gets resolved or reinforced. Jen wants confirmation, ideally in writing, of what was agreed, partly for her own peace of mind and partly to be able to update other family members accurately.

Touchpoints we control. The paperwork walkthrough; the payment itself (reported generally within 24 hours via OSKO); the pick-up arrangement. [HIGH-CONTROL] The precise payment-to-settlement sequence — including any finance-owing, identity or registration-transfer checks — is not documented in the source material and is pending direct confirmation with Adam (CQ04); this document references the fact that fast payment happens and does not describe or invent the underlying steps.

Moments of truth. Payment actually arriving on the timeline stated is the moment that converts "no pressure, no strangers, no hassle" from a promise into a proven experience — this is where the brand's credibility either compounds or is spent. This is a step where human reassurance still matters (a confirmation of what happens next, in plain language) even where back-office parts of it could reasonably be supported by better tooling.

Where the journey breaks today. No formal receipt or confirmation system exists beyond the transfer itself (confirmed in sales-process-review.html) — someone anxious about the process, especially Jen managing a family member's asset, has no written confirmation to point to afterwards. What happens when a seller doesn't decide on the spot, or a follow-up call goes unanswered, is not described anywhere and should not be assumed to be handled well simply because outcomes have been good to date.

Stage 6 — Advocacy and referral

Thinks / Feels / Does. A satisfied Barbara tells her family and friends, unprompted — testimonials already show this happens organically ("I will only sell my cars to Adam"). Jen, if the experience was handled with genuine care, becomes an especially credible advocate precisely because her situation was emotionally loaded — she is the segment most likely to talk about the experience, not just the outcome.

Touchpoints we control. Whether a referral is actively asked for at the point of highest goodwill (immediately after a well-handled sale), versus left to happen or not on its own.

Moments of truth. The referral ask itself, if it exists at all — and per core-questions-digest.html, whether Adam asks or it "just occurs" is explicitly unconfirmed (CQ07, CQ16). This is the single cheapest, highest-leverage unbuilt moment in the whole journey.

Where the journey breaks today. No referral is systematically asked for, tracked, or incentivised (confirmed in funnel-review.html) — the business is currently relying on organic word of mouth continuing to happen at the same rate it apparently has, without any mechanism to protect or grow it deliberately.

Journey verdict

The two breaks to fix first. First, Stage 3 (first contact) has zero capture and zero measurement — every enquiry's fate depends on Adam's memory alone, which means the business cannot currently see where it is losing people between "heard of Adam" and "had a home visit." A simple, human-compatible intake log (not a chatbot replacing the call, a record supporting it) is the highest-leverage single fix available and is already flagged as fix #2 in sales-process-review.html. Second, Stage 6 (advocacy) has no formal ask despite clear organic evidence that referral already happens — systemising a simple, Adam-authored ask (CQ16) at the point of highest goodwill is the cheapest volume lever in the entire journey, requiring no new spend and no new channel.

Signals worth watching. The clearest first-value moment in this journey is the home visit itself going well — everything upstream exists to earn it, and everything downstream depends on it. The clearest early churn signal would be a considered lead (someone who called but didn't book a visit) going quiet, which cannot currently be seen at all because it isn't captured — this is a measurement gap to close before it can be watched. The clearest advocacy trigger is a well-handled estate/health-driven sale, given how much more likely this segment is to become a vocal referral source when treated with genuine care, per the pattern in customer-segments.html.
