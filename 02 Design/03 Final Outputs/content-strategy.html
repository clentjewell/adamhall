Content Strategy

How to read this document

This is the content engine across every owned channel — website, blog/articles, the existing "Listen" radio archive, future email, and social — not a social media plan on its own. It builds on messaging-offer-architecture.html (the three confirmed message pillars), customer-profile.html and customer-journey.html (who we write for and where they are in the decision), and the Discover-phase evidence in customer-pain-points.html, buying-triggers-barriers.html and market-gaps.html. The per-platform expression of the pillars defined here lives in social-strategy.html, written in the same run — pillar thinking is done once, here.

Two flags carried forward from upstream documents. First, whether the brand permanently carries Adam's personal name (CQ11) is unresolved; every pillar and hero format below is written to work under the current name "Adam Hall Buy My Car" and would transfer unchanged if the brand later generalises to "The Adam Hall Standard" (brand-strategy.html). Second, nothing here states or implies the valuation logic, the "one number, never moved" guarantee, or the payment/settlement sequence as confirmed fact — those remain HIGH-CONTROL, pending Adam's direct confirmation (CQ01–CQ04), and content built against pillar 3 below must use "Real Market Pricing, explained plainly" rather than an unconditional guarantee until that gate clears.

The business currently produces almost no owned content beyond the five-page brochure site and the weekly 4CRB radio segment (audience-teardown.html). This plan is written for that reality — a near-zero content operation today — not for a business with an existing content team.

Content pillars

Five pillars: the three confirmed message pillars from messaging-offer-architecture.html, plus two pillars this engagement adds to carry Bet 4 in strategic-priorities.html — claiming the estate/health-crisis segment and the three-region geography. Every pillar traces to a message pillar and a customer pain; none is "thought leadership" for its own sake.

Pillar 1 — A Person, Not a Company

Message pillar: "It's always the same known person, from your first call to the day you're paid" (messaging-offer-architecture.html, pillar 1). Pain answered: fear of being ripped off; distrust of the anonymous "car buyer" category; discomfort with strangers, especially at home (customer-pain-points.html, pains 1 and 3). Rationale: named-person continuity is the one claim no researched competitor can make (competitor-analysis.html) — content exists to make Adam recognisable before he is ever needed, not to sell in the moment.

  • Meet Adam: 27 years in the trade, in his own words
  • How "What's Your Car Worth?" started, and what 10,000+ live valuations actually means
  • A day in the life of a home visit (from Adam's side, not a sales pitch)
  • "Why I don't work from a call centre" — the case for one person doing every visit
  • What a second, values-trained buyer will (and won't) change, once that work is confirmed
  • Reader/listener questions answered directly, radio-style, on the website

Pillar 2 — Relief, Not Another Task

Message pillar: "One phone call, one visit — we handle everything, so you don't have to" (messaging-offer-architecture.html, pillar 2). Pain answered: dread of the private-sale process; paperwork and technology anxiety; the emotional weight of a life-change sale (customer-pain-points.html, pains 2, 4 and 5). Rationale: this is the pillar that does the pre-selling work of answering objections before the phone call, per the Consideration stage in customer-journey.html.

  • What actually happens at a home visit, step by step, from the phone call to the goodbye
  • "Do I need to do anything before you arrive?" — the honest answer is no, and why
  • Us vs. private sale vs. dealership trade-in vs. an instant-offer website — a plain comparison
  • The paperwork, explained in advance so nothing is a surprise on the day
  • What happens after you say yes: payment and pick-up, in plain language (no HIGH-CONTROL specifics stated beyond what is publicly confirmed)
  • Reader FAQ: "will my car even be wanted?" answered directly, by car type

Pillar 3 — Honesty With Reasons

Message pillar: "A fair number, explained plainly, that doesn't change after we've already looked at the car" (messaging-offer-architecture.html, pillar 3). Pain answered: fear of a lowball offer once "committed"; uncertainty about whether the car is even wanted (customer-pain-points.html, pains 1 and 6). Rationale: this pillar answers the single most-repeated complaint against price-led competitors — the price moving after inspection (competitor-analysis.html) — but must stay inside what is provably true today. Flag: content here uses "Real Market Pricing, explained plainly" and "a number that's meant to hold"; it must never state an unconditional "guaranteed never to move" promise until Adam's valuation rules are documented and consistently kept (CQ01–CQ03, HIGH-CONTROL-adjacent, per positioning.html).

  • How a valuation actually works, in plain language (without disclosing proprietary judgement)
  • "Why I said no to this car" — an honesty piece that builds trust precisely by not always saying yes
  • Testimonials that name a fair, explained number, formatted as standalone proof pieces
  • Myths about car buyers, debunked one at a time
  • What "obligation-free" actually means, tested against a real scenario

Pillar 4 — Handled With Care (estate & health-crisis, hypothesis-piloted)

Message pillar: "We'll take care of it, so you don't have to" (messaging-offer-architecture.html, expansion offer 1). Pain answered: the emotional weight of selling a parent's or partner's car; fear the process will feel transactional during an already hard season (customer-profile.html, Jen persona). Rationale: this is Bet 4 in strategic-priorities.html — an open segment no researched competitor visibly serves (market-gaps.html). Flag: genuinely sensitive territory — every piece should be drafted gently and, wherever possible, in Adam's own words once captured (CQ13), reviewed before publishing, and never used to manufacture urgency around a death, illness or care transition.

  • "Selling a parent's car" — a short, practical guide for the adult child managing it
  • Conversation starters for families navigating a licence loss or care-home move together
  • What to expect if you're acting on someone else's behalf (do they need to be present, who signs what)
  • A gently anonymised testimonial modelled on the confirmed Currumbin Waters story, with fresh consent for any new piece
  • A simple checklist: what to do with a car during a downsizing or aged-care transition

Pillar 5 — Local and Real

Message pillar: named-person continuity extended to place — "we come to you, wherever you are" (positioning.html). Pain answered: distrust of a service that "isn't really local" or doesn't cover an area properly, especially the Northern Rivers gap named against We Buy Cars (positioning.html, alternatives table). Rationale: Bet 4's second open claim — Northern Rivers coverage no researched competitor visibly claims (market-gaps.html) — made concrete and provable rather than asserted.

  • Area spotlights: Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers, each with real local testimonials once volume supports it
  • "Yes, we come to the Northern Rivers" — a direct, simple answer to the coverage question
  • Recaps of recent 4CRB "What's Your Car Worth?" segments, formatted for the website's existing "Listen" page
  • Community presence write-ups once any local talks or events happen (cross-reference partnership-referral-plan.html)
  • A simple, honest coverage map or list of suburbs/towns served

The repurposing chain

One production effort should feed every channel. Two hero formats carry the chain, matched to what this business can realistically produce.

Hero 1 — the long-form website article/guide

  1. Full article published on the website (600–900 words, plain language, large type — see typography direction in brand-strategy.html)
  2. A FAQ entry extracted from its clearest answer
  3. Two to three Facebook posts: a quote graphic, a short "did you know" post, and a link-back post — see social-strategy.html for format and cadence
  4. A one-paragraph talking point handed to Adam for the radio segment, in his own voice
  5. Once email exists (flagged future step, not yet built — see market-gaps.html gap on CRM/nurture), a nurture email drawing on the same article for a lead who hasn't yet booked a visit

Hero 2 — the radio segment recording

  1. The existing 4CRB "What's Your Car Worth?" segment, as broadcast (no new production cost)
  2. A short recap posted to the website's existing "Listen" page
  3. An audio or quote clip shared to Facebook, captioned to explain the moment for anyone who wasn't listening live
  4. A testimonial or proof line lifted from the segment, formatted as a standalone graphic under Pillar 3

Both chains deliberately start from something Adam already does — writing or talking — rather than asking him to produce new raw material from a standing start, consistent with the capacity constraint in business-plan.html.

Cadence and capacity

This is a solo-operator business with zero dedicated marketing capacity today (business-plan.html, cost structure). The cadence below is deliberately conservative, and is explicitly sequenced after the capacity and measurement work in strategic-priorities.html Bets 1 and 2 — content production should not compete with Adam's time for CORE mapping sessions or home visits.

OutputWho makes itCadence
Hero article/guideMaxxim/agency drafts from a short interview or voice note with Adam; Adam reviews and approves1 per month (minimum viable — see below)
Radio recap and talking pointsMaxxim/agency, from the existing weekly segmentWeekly, no new production burden on Adam
Social posts (derivatives)Maxxim/agency, batched from the month's hero pieceSee social-strategy.html posting template
FAQ updatesMaxxim/agency, reviewed by Adam for anything HIGH-CONTROL-adjacentQuarterly, or as new objections surface
Pillar 4 (estate/health-crisis) piecesDrafted only once CQ13 gives Adam's own words; reviewed before publishingPiloted, not scheduled — quality over calendar

Minimum viable cadence: one hero piece a month is the floor. Below that, there is not enough raw material to feed the FAQ, the radio talking points and the social calendar in social-strategy.html at the same time, and the whole engine stalls rather than just slowing down. Cut the number of derivative social posts before cutting the hero piece itself — the hero is what feeds SEO and the radio talking points; the social posts are the cheapest thing to trim if capacity tightens further.

How content feeds SEO

The current five-page brochure site has no blog, no FAQ depth and no structured content for search or AI-assistant visibility (business-plan.html, Opportunities). Pillars 2, 3 and 5 are the natural keyword carriers: "how it works", "sell my car" objection and comparison content, and area/coverage content, respectively. Pillar 4 carries a distinct, lower-volume but high-intent search theme — "selling a parent's car", "car after aged care move" — that no researched competitor visibly targets (market-gaps.html). The detailed keyword plan and technical structure belong in seo-strategy.html once commissioned; this document supplies that plan with the pillar and topic list to map keywords against, rather than restating a keyword plan here.

How content feeds social

Every pillar above is written to be sliced into the formats social-strategy.html specifies for Facebook and the secondary video library — quote graphics, short testimonial clips, FAQ posts and area spotlights all originate from the hero pieces and radio recaps defined here. Social does not generate its own separate topic list; it draws down from this content calendar.
