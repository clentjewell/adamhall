Events Plan

How to read this document

This is the event strategy for Adam Hall Buy My Car, built on the partner types already identified in partnership-referral-plan.html, the segments and personas in customer-profile.html, and the channel and campaign sequencing set in marketing-plan.html and campaign-calendar.html. It turns the “local community organisation speaking slot” and “retirement village/downsizing-service partnership” recommendations already made there into the actual event mechanics — format, logistics, promotion, content capture and follow-up — without repeating the partner rationale already covered in full in partnership-referral-plan.html.

One constraint governs every recommendation below and is stated once, plainly: Adam’s own time is the business’s scarcest resource. The business runs at roughly 25 cars a month on one person’s capacity, with valuation judgement, customer trust and settlement discipline all living in Adam’s head and nowhere else (per the underlying discovery material). Every event recommended here is therefore chosen for high trust-leverage per hour of Adam’s time, or is structured so the logistics, promotion and content-capture work can be delegated to Maxxim/agency, leaving Adam to do only the one thing only he can do: show up and talk. No event in this plan asks for more than a handful of hours of Adam’s time in any given month, and this plan does not recommend scaling event cadence up until a second, values-trained buyer exists to share the load (per brand-strategy.html, Brand Architecture).

Budget is unconfirmed, as it is throughout this engagement (marketing-plan.html). This plan is scenario-scaled to the same Conservative and Moderate scenarios used there — no figure below should be read as a confirmed line item.

2. Event Strategy Overview

Recommended event types, and why:

  • Speaking engagements at existing community organisations (Probus, CWA, Rotary, U3A, RSL sub-branches). Highest-leverage option available: a single 20–30 minute talk reaches an entire room of the exact downsizer audience this business already serves, in a format these groups actively welcome (per partnership-referral-plan.html, partner rank 2). No venue cost, no promotion cost — the organisation does both.
  • Retirement village and downsizing-service touchpoints (an information session, a stall at a resident event, or simply being named on a village’s resource list). Lower time cost than a full talk in most cases, and reaches people already actively moving — the exact trigger moment for this segment (customer-profile.html).
  • Community sponsorship (a small, modest sponsorship of a Rotary or RSL fundraiser, or a local seniors’ group event). Low-cost, relationship-building, and consistent with the trust-first brand position — not a lead-generation mechanic in itself.
  • A modest presence at a genuinely relevant seniors’-focused community expo, if and when one is confirmed to run in the Gold Coast/Brisbane region during the engagement period. Treated as a Moderate-scenario, lower-priority option below, not a committed plan — no specific expo is named or confirmed here.

Event types explicitly not recommended, and why:

  • An owned, self-run public event (a launch, an open day, a large customer event). No evidence this audience wants or needs a self-run event when the far lower-cost, lower-effort option of speaking at an existing community gathering achieves the same trust-building goal; also a poor use of Adam’s scarce time relative to the return.
  • Large trade shows or multi-day consumer expos requiring sustained booth staffing. Directly conflicts with the one-person capacity constraint; a multi-day commitment would come straight out of home-visit and valuation time, which growth-roadmap.html and business-plan.html both name as the business’s real constraint.
  • Webinars or virtual events. No evidence in audience-teardown.html or customer-profile.html that this audience prefers or expects a virtual-event format over an in-person community gathering they may already attend; a webinar also loses the in-person warmth this brand’s personality depends on (brand-strategy.html). [Hypothesis — revisit only if direct evidence emerges that this audience wants it.]
  • Sponsoring events outside this audience (youth, family or general-community events with no clear downsizer, estate/health-crisis or licence-loss overlap). Audience mismatch; sponsorship budget, however modest, should follow the audience, not general community goodwill alone.

4. Owned Event Plans

No fully owned, self-run event is recommended in this Design phase (see Section 2). The one recurring “owned” format this plan does recommend is the community-organisation talk, which Adam Hall Buy My Car supplies the content and speaker for, even though the organisation supplies the venue and audience.

Format: the community talk

Concept and theme. “What your car is really worth, and how to avoid the common traps when selling privately” — genuinely useful, non-salesy community content, matching the offer already defined in partnership-referral-plan.html.

Format. In-person, 20–30 minutes plus open Q&A, delivered in Adam’s own conversational, radio-honed voice.

Agenda framework. A short introduction from the host organisation; the talk itself (what a car is really worth, common mistakes sellers make, honest answers to the audience’s real questions); open Q&A; an informal, no-pressure period afterwards for individual conversations — never a hard close or a group sign-up pitch, consistent with the brand’s no-pressure voice (brand-strategy.html).

Venue requirements. The host organisation’s own clubroom, hall or meeting space — no venue cost to the business.

Speaker and content needs. Adam only; no additional speaker or panel required. Content drawn directly from the message pillars already defined in content-strategy.html (Pillars 1 and 3 in particular), so no new material needs to be created from scratch for each talk.

Promotion plan. Almost entirely the host organisation’s own channels — its newsletter or meeting notice, typically circulated 2–4 weeks ahead. Adam Hall Buy My Car’s own channels are used for a light post-event recap, not pre-event promotion, since driving outside attendance to what is fundamentally a member meeting is neither appropriate nor useful.

Registration approach. None required — open attendance via the host organisation’s own membership, consistent with how these groups already operate.

Expected attendance. Typical Probus, CWA, Rotary and U3A chapter meetings commonly draw in the order of 20–40 attendees, based on the general format of these community groups — this is an assumption to confirm with the specific chapter before finalising any logistics, not a figure this engagement has verified. [Hypothesis.]

5. Sponsorship & Speaking Strategy

Selection criteria. Audience alignment with the downsizer and estate/health-driven segments first; near-zero or low cost second; ease of activation third — matching the ranking logic already set out in partnership-referral-plan.html’s partner leverage map. This plan does not re-rank those partner types; it only adds the event-specific mechanics for the two highest-ranked, easiest-to-activate types (community organisations and retirement villages/downsizing services).

Sponsorship tier recommendations. A modest, in-kind or small cash sponsorship of a single community-organisation fundraiser or newsletter (for example a Rotary or RSL sub-branch event) is a reasonable, low-cost way to deepen a relationship already built through a speaking engagement — the specific amount is unconfirmed and should sit inside the Moderate-scenario “local community/print-adjacent” budget line already described in marketing-plan.html, not as a separate new spend category.

Speaking topic proposals, tied to the brand’s authority areas (brand-strategy.html, content-strategy.html):

  • “What your car is really worth, and how to avoid the common traps when selling privately” — the core, repeatable talk (Pillar 1 and 3 territory).
  • “Selling a parent’s car” — a gentler, more specific talk suited to any organisation whose members are actively supporting family through an estate or health transition (Pillar 4 territory); this topic should only be offered once Adam’s own words on handling these situations have been captured (CQ13, per content-strategy.html).

Expected return. Relationship equity and warm, trackable referral leads (tagged by source in the lead log, per measurement-plan.html) rather than a large volume of leads from any single engagement — consistent with this being a trust channel, not a volume channel.

6. Event Promotion

Promotion timeline. For community-organisation talks, promotion is almost entirely the host organisation’s own newsletter or meeting notice, typically 2–4 weeks ahead — this plan does not add a separate, business-run promotion campaign on top of that, since doing so would risk turning a low-pressure community talk into something that reads as a marketing push.

Channel strategy. Facebook and the website (once rebuilt) are used for a light post-event recap only — a short write-up and a photo or two, feeding content-strategy.html Pillar 5 — not for pre-event ticket-selling or urgency-driven promotion.

Registration funnel design. None required for community talks (open attendance via the host organisation). If a seniors’ expo booth is pursued (Moderate scenario, Section 3), no registration funnel is needed either — expo attendance is managed by the expo organiser, not this business.

Early-bird and urgency tactics. Explicitly not recommended for any event in this plan. Urgency and scarcity tactics directly contradict the calm, no-pressure brand voice this business is built on (brand-strategy.html) and would be a poor fit for an audience this plan is trying to build trust with, not convert quickly.

Attendance target and conversion assumptions. No confirmed conversion assumption exists for this business’s events — there is no history to draw one from. [Hypothesis, no baseline.] Once the first talk runs and is tagged in the lead log, a working assumption can be set from real data rather than an industry rule of thumb.

7. Attendee Experience

For a community-organisation talk. Confirmation happens informally through the host organisation’s own meeting notice, not a separate confirmation email (none is needed or appropriate for an open community meeting). On the day, the host organisation typically introduces Adam; Adam delivers the talk in his own conversational voice; the open Q&A and informal post-talk conversation period are the most important touchpoints, since this is where individual trust actually builds. No forced networking facilitation is needed — members of these groups typically already know each other. Branded elements should be minimal and understated (a simple printed flyer or business card, available but not pushed), consistent with the “never a hard sell” brand direction. Post-event communication is limited to the recap content described below; there is no automated post-event email sequence, since no email/CRM system exists yet (content-strategy.html, marketing-plan.html).

For a retirement village/downsizing-service touchpoint. The journey is largely handled through the partner’s own resident communications (a welcome pack mention, a noticeboard flyer, or a brief information session as part of a broader resident event) — Adam Hall Buy My Car’s role is to be a reliable, no-pressure presence the village is comfortable recommending, not to run a separate attendee journey of its own.

For any Moderate-scenario expo booth. Simple, warm signage and a printed flyer with a clear, obligation-free call to action (a free valuation, no pressure to sell); the booth should be staffed by Adam personally wherever possible, since the entire brand promise depends on the same known person being present — if Adam cannot staff it personally, this plan does not recommend proceeding with the booth that period rather than sending an unbranded stand-in.

9. Lead Capture & Follow-Up

Registration data collection. Minimal by design — most events in this plan require no formal registration. Where a simple, optional “leave your name for a free valuation chat” sheet or card is offered at a talk, it should be framed as entirely optional and low-pressure, consistent with the brand voice.

In-event interaction tracking. A simple headcount and a note of any specific enquiries raised during or after the talk, recorded manually by Adam into the lead log described in measurement-plan.html — no dedicated event-tracking technology is proposed, since none is justified at this scale.

Lead scoring adjustments. No formal lead-scoring system exists today (no CRM, per marketing-plan.html); event-sourced leads should simply be tagged by source (for example “event — [organisation name]”) in the lead log so their volume and conversion can be reviewed once enough data exists.

Post-event nurture sequence. No email nurture channel exists yet — this is a named, flagged gap in marketing-plan.html and content-strategy.html, not an oversight. Until one exists, the realistic follow-up is a personal phone call from Adam within a few days of the event to anyone who left contact details, matching the personal, unhurried brand voice rather than an automated sequence.

CRM integration requirements. None exist today. Event-sourced lead data should be captured in whatever simple lead log or call register measurement-plan.html establishes, ready to migrate into a CRM once one is commissioned (crm-plan.html, once that document exists).

Sales handoff criteria. Not applicable in the traditional sense — Adam is a solo operator who handles every stage personally. There is no handoff to a separate sales team; this should be revisited only once a second buyer is trained and taking on some of this load.
