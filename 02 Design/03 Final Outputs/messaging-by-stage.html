Messaging by Stage

How to read this document

This is the working brief for the copy team: what to say at each of the six stages defined in customer-journey.html, for the two priority personas named in customer-profile.html — Barbara (downsizer) and Jen (estate/health-driven family seller). Every message below traces to a specific pain in customer-pain-points.html or a trigger and barrier in buying-triggers-barriers.html — nothing here is invented tone, it is evidenced pain and objection turned into words. The ask at each stage matches what the journey document says actually happens there; a Decision-stage ask never appears at an Exposure-stage moment.

Stage 1 — Exposure

Job of the message. Plant a specific, trustworthy name — not a category pitch. Make Adam recognisable before he is ever needed.

Core message. "A real person, not a company, buys cars across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers — and you've probably already heard him." Support points: 27+ years in the trade; a decade of live, on-air valuations on 4CRB's "What's Your Car Worth?"; buys direct, at your door, no middleman.

Proof to show. The 4CRB relationship itself (10,000+ live valuations since 2010) — this is proof of familiarity, not a claim that needs defending.

Call-to-action. None, deliberately — this stage earns recognition, not a call. The only "ask" is remembering a name.

Traced to. Answers the category-level distrust of anonymous "car buyer" services (customer-pain-points.html, pain #1 and #10) by making the alternative a specific, named person before it is ever a sales pitch.

Stage 2 — Consideration

Job of the message. Pre-empt the three objections this audience is too polite, too unsure or too private to ask out loud, so the decision to call feels lower-risk than it currently does.

Core message. "Free, obligation-free, no catch." Support points: we buy all car types, so you're not wasting anyone's time by calling; the valuation costs nothing and commits you to nothing; you decide, with no pressure either way.

Proof to show. Named testimonials that describe a no-pressure experience (e.g. the pattern behind "I will only sell my cars to Adam"); the FAQ answering "will my car be wanted" directly.

Call-to-action. A low-commitment first step: "Call and find out — it costs you nothing to ask." Not "book now," which overstates readiness at this stage.

Traced to. Directly answers barriers #1, #2 and #3 in buying-triggers-barriers.html ("is the valuation really free," "am I obligated to sell," "will my car even be wanted") and pain #10 in customer-pain-points.html (not knowing what "obligation-free" really means).

Stage 3 — First contact

Job of the message. Confirm, within the first exchange, that the person on the phone is exactly who was expected from the radio or the referral — and make the very first step feel trivial, not like the start of a project.

Core message. "Tell me about the car — I'll let you know honestly whether it's a fit, no pressure either way." Support points: no need to prepare the car, gather documents, or take photos before calling; a straightforward yes/no on fit, given quickly; the same person handling the call as will attend the visit.

Proof to show. Consistency — the tone here should be identical to the radio segment's tone, so recognition (built in Stage 1) is confirmed rather than contradicted.

Call-to-action. Book a home visit at a time that suits the seller — the first genuinely committing ask in the journey, and appropriately so, since intent is now established.

Traced to. Answers pain #6 (uncertainty about whether their car is even wanted) and barrier #8 (inertia — "I'll get to it eventually") from buying-triggers-barriers.html, by making the first step feel small rather than effortful.

Stage 4 — Home visit and decision

Job of the message. Build belief, in person, that the number offered is fair and explained — not delivered and defended. This is the trust moment; the message here is carried by conduct as much as words.

Core message. "Here's what I'd pay, and here's exactly why." Support points: Real Market Pricing, explained in plain language, not jargon; no pressure to decide on the spot; a genuinely available "no," with nothing lost by saying it.

Proof to show. The explanation itself, delivered clearly and without rush — the strongest proof at this stage is the seller's own understanding of how the number was reached, not a printed claim.

Call-to-action. A clear yes/no decision, genuinely open either way — this is the one stage in the whole journey where the correct ask is explicitly "or say no, with nothing lost."

Traced to. Directly answers pain #1 (fear of being ripped off) and barrier #4 (fear of a lowball offer once "committed") from customer-pain-points.html and buying-triggers-barriers.html — the single most-repeated fear across all three segments.

Stage 5 — Settlement and pick-up

Job of the message. De-risk the yes just given — confirm, plainly, that what happens next is straightforward and fast, without describing any part of the payment or paperwork sequence that has not been confirmed with Adam directly (HIGH-CONTROL, pending CQ04).

Core message. "You're taken care of from here." Support points: payment generally within 24 hours via fast bank transfer; the paperwork is walked through, not left for the seller to work out alone; pick-up is free, arranged at a time that suits.

Proof to show. Testimonials describing the payment actually arriving as promised (e.g. the pattern behind "the money was in my account the same day").

Call-to-action. Confirm pick-up time — a light, practical ask, appropriately small for a stage where the seller has already committed.

Traced to. Answers Job 6 in jobs-to-be-done.html (certainty the paperwork is right) and pain #4 (paperwork and technology anxiety) from customer-pain-points.html, without overstating anything about the specific settlement mechanics that remain pending confirmation.

Stage 6 — Advocacy and referral

Job of the message. Ask, plainly and warmly, for the referral that the evidence suggests already happens on its own — turning organic goodwill into a repeatable behaviour rather than leaving it to chance.

Core message. "If this was a good experience, I'd really appreciate you telling someone else who might need the same thing." Support points: framed as a genuine ask, not a scripted upsell; timed for the moment goodwill is highest, immediately after a well-handled sale; no pressure, consistent with the brand's whole promise.

Proof to show. None needed — this stage is proof-neutral; the seller's own good experience is the only proof required to make the ask land.

Call-to-action. "Know someone in a similar spot? Pass on my number." A specific, low-friction referral action rather than a vague "tell your friends."

Traced to. Directly answers the systemisation gap named in funnel-review.html and sales-process-review.html — the referral ask is confirmed as unbuilt (CQ07, CQ16), and this message exists to close that gap the moment Adam confirms his own wording.

Message spine

Every stage should sound like the same person talking, because it is: a specific, known individual with 27 years in the trade, who explains a fair number honestly, asks nothing of the seller they haven't already been told plainly to expect, and makes it genuinely, provably safe to say no at every step until the moment they choose to say yes. The thread that must survive from radio segment to home visit to referral ask is "the number never moves" — the promise that what is offered is what is paid, with no post-inspection surprise, which is also the sharpest gap against price-led competitors named in category-positioning.html and differentiation-opportunities.html. Nothing in this spine should ever be voiced by anything other than a human at the moments that matter most — first contact and the home visit — even as intake, records and follow-up are supported behind the scenes by better tools; see conversion-pathway.html for where that human/tooling line is drawn in practice.
