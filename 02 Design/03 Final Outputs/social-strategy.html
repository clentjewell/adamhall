Social Strategy

How to read this document

This is the platform playbook — where to show up, what to post, how often, and how organic and paid work together. The pillars it expresses are defined once in content-strategy.html, written in the same run; this document does not redefine them. It builds on audience-teardown.html, customer-profile.html and competitor-analysis.html for who the audience is, where they actually spend attention, and what competitors currently do on social (largely nothing distinctive — see Platform Strategy below).

The starting reality, stated plainly: the primary audience is aged 60–75+ and is confirmed, not assumed, to be reached today through 4CRB radio and organic word of mouth — not through TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat or LinkedIn (audience-teardown.html; USER.html). The one adjacent audience that does behave differently is the adult children (roughly 40–55) who influence or decide for the estate/health-driven segment (customer-profile.html, Jen persona) — they are reachable on Facebook and Google, and their information-seeking behaviour includes general search and, increasingly, AI assistants (discover-summary.html). This plan is built around that evidence, not around what is fashionable in social media generally. There is currently no active social presence, no follower base and no historical engagement data to benchmark against (audience-teardown.html) — every target below is a directional first estimate, flagged as a hypothesis to be replaced with a real baseline within the first reporting cycle.

2. Platform strategy

Facebook — primary platform

Why this platform. Facebook is where this audience — and the adult children who influence the estate/health-crisis segment — actually spend time, unlike Instagram, TikTok or LinkedIn (audience-teardown.html). It is also the one platform where local community groups (suburb "buy/swap/sell" and community noticeboard groups) let a business build word-of-mouth density organically, matching the "always-on" channel affinity named for downsizers in customer-profile.html.

Audience overlap. Barbara (downsizer) and her adult children both use Facebook, if at different intensities; Jen (estate/health-driven family seller) is a highly plausible Facebook user in the 40–55 age band and is the segment most likely to search or scroll for "how do I sell a parent's car" content. Graham (licence-loss) is a lower-priority audience here, captured opportunistically rather than targeted (customer-profile.html).

Best content formats. Single images with warm, real photography (Adam, driveways, real cars — never stock or showroom imagery, per brand-strategy.html); short native video (60–90 seconds) for testimonials and explainer content once capacity allows; text-led "did you know" or FAQ posts, which perform well with an older audience that reads before it watches; shared/reposted local news or community content that keeps the Page feeling local rather than corporate.

Posting cadence. Conservative: 2 posts per week. Moderate (once a second buyer or agency support exists): 3–4 posts per week. Rationale: this matches the "minimum viable cadence" set in content-strategy.html and avoids posting for its own sake — an older audience notices low-value filler content more, not less, than a younger one.

Tone adaptation. The brand voice on Facebook stays proof-led rather than open-ended chat — testimonials, plain answers to objections in buying-triggers-barriers.html, and the same warm, unhurried register as the radio segment (brand-strategy.html, channel-specific tone adaptations).

Competitive landscape. No researched competitor (CarBuyers, We Buy Cars, carsales Instant Offer) shows a distinctive, trust-led local social presence (competitor-analysis.html) — their category defaults to price-led, high-urgency creative. The opportunity is a calm, local, named-person presence that looks nothing like a "we buy any car" ad.

YouTube — secondary, video library only

Why this platform, and how it differs from Facebook. YouTube is used here as a hosting library for testimonial and explainer video, embedded on the website to support Objective 4 (AI-chatbot and generative-search visibility), not as an actively grown subscriber channel. Cadence: quarterly uploads, matched to whenever a hero video is produced (content-strategy.html, repurposing chain) — this is deliberately low-frequency and should not be treated as a platform requiring its own weekly content plan.

Platforms to skip

  • Instagram. The primary audience (60–75+) is not meaningfully present here, and the adult-children audience's use of it skews toward younger, more visual/lifestyle content than this category calls for. Format mismatch and resource cost both argue against it while capacity is this constrained.
  • TikTok. Confirmed audience mismatch — this is explicitly not where the 60–75+ primary audience spends attention (audience-teardown.html), and short-form trend content sits awkwardly against a trust-first, unhurried brand voice.
  • LinkedIn. This is a B2C, home-visit consumer service; there is no professional-audience rationale for LinkedIn investment at this stage.
  • X/Twitter and Snapchat. No audience overlap identified in any Discover document; skip entirely.
  • Paid national or category-style social ad formats (e.g. flashy price-led carousels). Even on Facebook, format and tone must never borrow the "act now" urgency style used by CarBuyers or We Buy Cars (brand-strategy.html) — this is a content and creative discipline, not a platform choice, but it rules out an entire register of ad formats regardless of platform.

4. Content mix and formats

  • Static images (~50% of posts, conservative-capacity priority). The lowest-cost format to produce well; real photography of Adam, homes and driveways across the three regions. Use case: everyday feed presence, testimonial quote graphics, "did you know" facts.
  • Short native video (~25% of posts, capacity-dependent). Testimonials and short explainers; the format to grow into once a second buyer or agency support frees Adam's time, not a month-1 requirement.
  • Text-led posts (~15%). FAQ answers and myth-busting; this older audience reads plain text well and it is the cheapest format to produce.
  • Shared/curated local content (~10%). Community and area-relevant shares that keep the Page feeling like a real local presence rather than a broadcast channel.
  • Carousels, Stories, polls. Not prioritised. Carousels have some use for the "how it works" step-by-step content; Stories and polls are lower priority given this audience's more passive feed-scrolling behaviour and are not recommended until the conservative cadence above is comfortably sustained.

Given the client's near-zero current production capacity (content-strategy.html, Cadence and capacity), static images and text-led posts should carry the bulk of the calendar in the first two quarters; video is added only once capacity work in strategic-priorities.html Bet 1 is progressing.

At moderate cadence (3–4/week), add a mid-week Pillar 3 testimonial post and, where relevant, a Pillar 4 post drawn from a reviewed, low-frequency queue rather than a fixed weekly slot.

Batching approach. One monthly batching session, timed to when the month's hero content piece (content-strategy.html) is finalised — social posts are derivatives of that piece, not separately researched or written. This avoids asking Adam for fresh raw material every week.

Key dates and moments. Cross-reference campaign-calendar.html for the quarterly themes and seasonal moments this calendar should flex around; this document does not repeat the year view.

Evergreen vs. timely split. Roughly 80% evergreen (testimonials, how-it-works, FAQ, area content) to 20% timely (a seasonal moment, a campaign launch, a radio segment recap) — this category has limited genuine newsjacking value for a 60–75+ audience, and evergreen trust content compounds in a way timely content does not.

6. Community management

Response-time targets. Conservative (no dedicated support): within 24 hours, same business day where possible. Moderate (once a second buyer or support role exists): 4–6 hours during business hours. Facebook is the only platform in active use, so this single target covers the whole plan.

Tone for replies. Warm, personal and unhurried, matching the brand voice guide in brand-strategy.html — replies should read like Adam, not like a support script. Never haggling or negotiation language, consistent with the tone rules in messaging-offer-architecture.html.

Escalation triggers. Any comment describing a price that "moved" after a visit, any complaint touching payment, settlement, PPSR or identity, and any comment from a visibly distressed estate/health-crisis commenter must be escalated to Adam directly and immediately — these are HIGH-CONTROL-adjacent or emotionally sensitive by definition (USER.html) and must never be handled by a general social-reply workflow. A clear sales opportunity (someone asking "do you buy X car") should be moved to a phone call as fast as possible, per the "phone over form" preference confirmed in customer-profile.html.

UGC approach. Encourage and curate, never force. Ask permission explicitly before reusing any customer comment or story as a testimonial post, particularly for Pillar 4 content, where consent matters more than reach.

Proactive engagement tactics. Genuine, non-promotional participation in local community Facebook groups (answering a relevant question honestly, not posting an ad into the group) is the single highest-value proactive tactic given this audience's channel behaviour — see market development in business-plan.html. Community-group posting rules must be respected; this is relationship-building, not a paid-media substitute.

7. Influencer and partnership strategy

Traditional micro-influencer or brand-collaboration marketing is not recommended for this business. The 60–75+ primary audience does not discover trust through influencer endorsement — it discovers trust through a known, named person and word of mouth (audience-teardown.html), which is a fundamentally different mechanism to influencer marketing and should not be forced to look like one.

What is relevant instead is community-figure advocacy — respected local voices (a seniors' club president, a retirement village coordinator) who can vouch for Adam within their own community, which is a partnership and referral mechanism, not a social-content one. The full partner program — types, offers, and first outreach moves — is a dedicated document: see partnership-referral-plan.html. This document does not duplicate it.

8. Paid social integration

Sequencing rule, carried from marketing-plan.html: no paid social spend should begin until the measurement baseline in measurement-plan.html exists (at least one full month of lead-log data) and capacity work is under way, per Bets 1 and 2 in strategic-priorities.html. Paid social is not a Now-stage activity in growth-roadmap.html.

Boost criteria. Only boost organic posts that have already proven themselves — a testimonial or proof-asset post with above-average organic engagement is the only category worth boosting; never boost an untested post purely for reach.

Dark post strategy. Once the Next stage begins, test unpublished ("dark") ad creative aimed specifically at the adult-children audience (roughly 35–55, in the three-region service area) around the Pillar 4 message — "help handling a parent's or partner's car" — since this audience segment is the one most plausibly reached by paid Facebook targeting rather than organic radio/referral recognition.

Audience targeting. Two audiences only, both geo-fenced to the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers postcodes: (1) 55+ for the downsizer message, matched to Barbara's decision criteria in customer-profile.html; (2) 35–55 for the estate/health-crisis message, matched to Jen. No broader lookalike or interest-based targeting is recommended until enough conversion data exists to build one responsibly.

Budget allocation. [Hypothesis, scenario-scaled — budget is unconfirmed pending Adam, per marketing-plan.html] Conservative scenario: $0 paid social; organic only. Moderate scenario: a small monthly test budget (illustrative range only, see marketing-plan.html Budget allocation) once the Next stage begins, split roughly two-thirds to the downsizer audience and one-third to the estate/health-crisis audience, reflecting relative volume versus advocacy value in customer-profile.html.

Testing approach. Start with one message, one audience, one small budget for a full month before adding a second variable — this audience is small in absolute reach terms compared to a mass-market Facebook campaign, so testing must be patient rather than run like a high-volume A/B program.

Tools. Native Facebook/Meta Page Insights and YouTube Studio to start; a social scheduling tool is not justified at the conservative posting cadence above and should only be considered once the moderate cadence is sustained for several months.

Optimisation triggers. If a testimonial-style post consistently outperforms other formats, shift the content mix toward more of it before adding new formats. If community-group engagement surfaces a complaint pattern, escalate per section 6 above rather than treating it as a metrics problem. If reach stays flat for three consecutive months despite consistent posting, revisit whether Facebook organic alone is sufficient or whether the Next-stage paid test should be brought forward.
