Brand Copy Workbook

How to use this workbook

This is the copy foundation for every downstream piece of writing — ads, the website rebuild, social posts, email, sales conversation scripts. It is built on brand-strategy.html, positioning.html and messaging-offer-architecture.html, and grounded in the real pain points and testimonials already gathered in customer-pain-points.html and website-crawl-digest.html. Voice throughout: honest, direct, warm, and under zero sales pressure — written in plain, larger-type-friendly language for an audience aged 60–75+, in Australian English.

Two rules that override everything else in this workbook:

  • “One number” claims stay soft. Never write “guaranteed”, “never changes” or any unconditional promise about the valuation not moving. Adam’s valuation decision rules are not yet documented and consistently proven (CQ01–CQ03, this is high-control adjacent per differentiation-opportunities.html and messaging-offer-architecture.html). Use “a number that’s meant to hold”, “explained plainly, not moved around” or “real market pricing, explained honestly” until Adam confirms the guarantee can be made in absolute terms.
  • No invented testimonials or statistics. Every quote and stat in this workbook is real and sourced from website-crawl-digest.html, customer-pain-points.html or messaging-offer-architecture.html. Do not fabricate a new testimonial, number or named customer for any future piece of content — if a claim needs a proof point that doesn’t exist yet, flag it as a gap rather than inventing one (see Proof Points & Social Proof, below).

Primary message. Adam Hall Buy My Car lets you sell your car from home, to one person you can trust, for a fair number explained in plain language — with the money in your account within a day.

Supporting messages

  • Trust — it’s always the same known person, not a company.
    • Adam personally attends every visit — never a rotating rep, call centre or dealer panel.
    • 27+ years in the automotive trade; former dealer principal representing Toyota, Ford, Holden, Honda, Mitsubishi, KIA and Suzuki.
    • “I will only sell my cars to Adam” — Rex, Kyogle.
  • Relief — one call, one visit, and it’s handled.
    • Free, obligation-free home-visit valuation — no cleaning, listing, advertising or fielding messages from strangers.
    • Paperwork explained in plain language, on the spot; complimentary car pick-up once a sale is agreed.
    • “I’ll never go back to selling a car privately again” — Terry, Pottsville.
  • Honesty — a number explained, not just handed down.
    • “Real Market Pricing” already stated plainly on the current site — no pricing games, no post-inspection drop.
    • Cars that aren’t a fit are told so straight away, rather than wasting the seller’s time.
    • Proof-pending: a public “number that’s meant to hold” promise, once Adam’s valuation rules are confirmed and consistently kept.
  • Proven — the credibility to back all of the above.
    • 10,000+ live, on-air valuations since 2010 as host of “What’s Your Car Worth?” on 4CRB 89.3FM.
    • 27+ years in the trade, 40+ years as a Gold Coast local.
    • Named testimonials spanning the Gold Coast, Brisbane hinterland and Northern Rivers.

2. Value proposition statement

“We come to you, tell you honestly what your car is worth, and if you want to sell, you’ll have the money in your account within a day — no pressure, no strangers, no hassle.”

This framing was chosen over alternatives because it answers the three things this audience actually needs to hear, in the order they need to hear them: who is coming (a known person, not a stranger), what happens (an honest number, explained), and what it costs them to find out (nothing — no obligation, no pressure). It is also the confirmed promise already stated in offer-worksheet.html, so every other message in this workbook builds on something already true rather than a new claim.

3. Key messages by audience

Downsizers

Primary message. No haggling, no strangers, just a fair number, at your door.

  • You’ve already decided to simplify — selling the car shouldn’t be the complicated part.
  • 27 years in the trade, and a name you may already know from 4CRB radio.
  • The valuation is free and obligation-free — you can hear the number with no pressure to say yes.
  • No listing, no photographing, no fielding calls from strangers.

Tone adjustments. Practical and unhurried — this seller is making a considered lifestyle decision, not reacting to a crisis. Lead with the process and the credibility, not the emotion.

Licence-loss sellers

Primary message. Sell this week, without the stress — no judgement, just a fair, fast outcome.

  • We come to you, so you don’t have to arrange transport or ask someone else to help.
  • No drawn-out process — one visit, and it’s largely done.
  • You decide if and when you’re ready — there is no obligation to sell on the day of the valuation.
  • A fair number, explained plainly, with the money in your account fast once you decide to go ahead.

Tone adjustments. Extra gentle around the “why” — never reference the licence change directly unless the customer raises it first; more empathetic, less brisk, no reference to speed as the headline benefit (it can read as rushing someone through a hard moment).

Estate/health-driven family sellers

Primary message. We’ll take care of it, so you don’t have to — handled with the same care Adam would want for his own family.

  • “My father is unwell and finds it difficult to leave the house. Dad was so grateful Adam was able to help.” — Jen, Currumbin Waters.
  • One phone call and it’s off your list — no need to coordinate anything beyond that first conversation.
  • Everything is explained clearly, at whatever pace works for your family, with no pressure to move faster than feels right.
  • Paperwork is handled in plain language, not left for you to interpret alone during an already difficult time.

Tone adjustments. Most empathetic and most patient of the three — this is the segment carrying the most emotional weight (customer-pain-points.html, pain 5 and pain 8). Never mention price first; acknowledge the situation before any commercial detail. Language should be drawn from Adam’s own words about how he currently handles these situations once available (differentiation-opportunities.html, CQ13) — this is a starting direction, not a finished script.

4. Headline & hook framework

AIDA (Attention–Interest–Desire–Action)

Template: [Attention-grabbing truth about the category] + [Interest: what’s different here] + [Desire: the relief this gives you] + [Action: the easy next step].

Example: “Selling a car usually means strangers, haggling and waiting for a call back. Adam comes to your home, gives you one honest number, and there’s no obligation to say yes. Most people tell us it’s the easiest part of a hard week. Call Adam on 0404 290 617 for your free valuation.”

PAS (Problem–Agitate–Solve)

Template: [Name the problem] + [Sit with how it actually feels] + [The solve, in plain terms].

Example: “Selling privately means photographing the car, fielding messages from people you’ve never met, and hoping they turn up. It’s exhausting before you’ve even agreed a price. Adam comes to you instead — one visit, one honest number, no strangers to manage.”

Benefit-led

Template: [The outcome, stated plainly] + [How it happens].

Example: “Sell your car without leaving your driveway. Adam visits, values it honestly, and if you’re happy to go ahead, the money’s in your account within a day.”

Curiosity-gap

Template: [An open loop the reader wants closed] — [the plain-language answer, not a trick].

Example: “There’s a reason people say ‘there really is nothing else like this in the market.’ It’s not a discount, and it’s not a gimmick — it’s one person, showing up, telling you the truth about what your car’s worth.”

Social proof

Template: [A real customer’s words] + [what it tells the reader about their own situation].

Example: “‘I will only sell my cars to Adam’ — Rex, Kyogle. After 27 years in the trade and 10,000+ live valuations on 4CRB radio, Rex isn’t the only one who feels that way.”

5. Tagline options

Carried from brand-strategy.html, with usage guidance sharpened for copy application. Per that document, taglines are written to be as CQ11-neutral as possible — usable regardless of how the brand-naming decision (CQ11) ultimately resolves — with one exception flagged clearly below.

“One visit. One number. No games.”

Rationale. Leads with the “number that’s meant to hold” differentiator without over-promising; reinforces directness and honesty. CQ11-neutral — names the promise, not the person.

Best use. Homepage hero, signage, radio sign-off, brand book cover — anywhere with a long shelf life.

“We come to you. We don’t move the goalposts.”

Rationale. Directly answers the category’s most-cited complaint (post-inspection price drops). CQ11-neutral.

Best use. Paid ads, comparison-style content, objection-handling pages.

“27 years in the trade. One promise that doesn’t change.”

Rationale. Leans on the credibility asset rather than the personal name specifically — transferable if the brand later generalises toward “The Adam Hall Standard.” CQ11-neutral.

Best use. About/credibility pages, sales collateral, print ads.

“Sell your car without a single stranger in the room.”

Rationale. Pain-first; names the emotional relief directly. Fully CQ11-neutral.

Best use. Social posts, email subject lines, the estate/health-crisis service track.

“The name you already trust, at your kitchen table.”

Rationale. Strongest tagline available under the current name, but explicitly not CQ11-neutral — it depends on the brand permanently carrying Adam’s personal name. Use freely in near-term, personal-name-era marketing; do not lock it into permanent assets (vehicle signage, a domain name, a registered trademark) until CQ11 is answered.

Best use. Near-term radio and social creative only — avoid signage, trademark filings or anything with a multi-year shelf life.

6. Tone of voice guide

Formality — plain, everyday language, never corporate or legalistic

Do: “We’ll come to you and tell you honestly what your car’s worth.” / “Give us a call and we’ll talk it through.”

Don’t: “Our valuation methodology delivers a market-competitive offer at your convenience.” / “Please refer to our terms of engagement for further detail.”

Confidence — quietly certain, backed by evidence, never boastful

Do: “I’ve valued cars live on the radio for over a decade — this is the same number I’d give you there.” / “27 years in the trade means I can usually tell you straight away if your car’s a fit.”

Don’t: “We guarantee the best price in Australia, no questions asked!” / “Nobody does it better than us.”

Warmth — personal, present-tense care, never forced cheer

Do: “Take your time — there’s no pressure to decide today.” / “I understand this isn’t always an easy call to make.”

Don’t: “We can’t wait to make you an amazing offer!” / “You’re going to LOVE this deal!”

Directness — says what it means, including “no”, never hedges

Do: “That one’s not a fit for us — I’d try [x] instead, so you’re not waiting on me.” / “Here’s the number, and here’s why.”

Don’t: “We may be able to consider your vehicle subject to a range of factors we’ll discuss further.” / “Terms and conditions apply.”

Channel-specific tone adaptations

Website (homepage, service pages). Warm and plain, but proof-led — testimonials, the radio credibility, and clear answers to common concerns, in larger, easy-to-read type. Every page keeps a single, obvious call to action (the phone number), matching the current site’s pattern.

Email (nurture, transactional). First-person from Adam where possible (“I’ll call you Thursday to confirm…”); transactional emails (payment confirmation, pick-up scheduling) stay plain and reassuring, never templated-sounding or automated-feeling.

Social media. The most human and story-led channel — real customer moments (with permission), the radio segment, Adam’s own voice. Avoid hard-sell captions, countdowns or urgency language entirely.

Sales collateral (flyers, signage, print). Confident and credibility-led — the 27 years, the radio show, the service area — with the phone number as the single, unmissable call to action, consistent with the current brand voice guidance to avoid negotiation or haggling language.

Adam Hall Buy My Car is a home-visit car-buying service across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers — a free, honest valuation, no pressure, no strangers.

50-word version

Adam Hall Buy My Car is a home-visit car-buying service for the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers. Adam personally visits, gives a free, obligation-free valuation, and if you’re happy to sell, arranges fast payment and complimentary pick-up — no haggling, no strangers, no pressure to decide on the day.

100-word version

Adam Hall Buy My Car is a home-visit, direct car-buying service across the Gold Coast, Brisbane and Northern Rivers, built around one person rather than a company or a sales panel. Adam personally visits sellers at home, gives a free, obligation-free valuation, and explains the number honestly — there’s never any pressure to accept it. If a seller decides to go ahead, Adam arranges fast payment and complimentary pick-up, usually within a day. With 27+ years in the automotive trade and a decade hosting “What’s Your Car Worth?” on 4CRB 89.3FM, Adam brings the same honest, plain-spoken approach to every home visit that his radio audience already knows.

Trust signals

  • 4CRB 89.3FM “What’s Your Car Worth?” radio segment, since 2010.
  • Former dealer principal and shareholder, having represented Toyota, Ford, Holden, Honda, Mitsubishi, KIA and Suzuki.
  • Existing on-site badges: “Hassle-free Guarantee”, “Real Market Pricing”, “Same Day Payment”, “5 Star Reviews”.
  • Gap to close: no independent review-platform presence has been established yet (positioning.html) — until it is, the brand’s proof rests on the testimonials above rather than public review volume.

Usage guidance

Stats (27+ years, 10,000+ valuations) work best in headlines and credibility sections, where a number earns attention quickly. Testimonials work best placed near a CTA, at the point a seller is deciding whether to make the first call. Trust badges belong in footers and near forms, as quiet reassurance rather than a headline claim. Never combine an unproven claim (the “guarantee” language) with a real stat in the same sentence — keep confirmed facts and soft, in-progress promises visibly distinct.
