Customer Profile

How to read this document

Discover analysed the audience in audience-teardown.html, customer-segments.html, customer-pain-points.html, buying-triggers-barriers.html and jobs-to-be-done.html. This document decides who we build for. It stays deliberately consistent with those five documents — where it commits to a call, it says so and why. Everything here sits on top of the three named segments (downsizers, licence-loss sellers, estate/health-driven family sellers) that are themselves still D06 hypotheses pending Adam's 22 CORE questions, none of which are answered yet. Treat every persona detail, objection and preference below as the working brief for copy, creative and web — sharp enough to build from, honest about what is still assumption. The stage-by-stage journey lives in customer-journey.html; this document does not repeat it.

The targeting call

This engagement commits first to two segments: Downsizers as the primary target, and Estate/health-driven family sellers as the priority secondary target. This follows the priority call already made in customer-segments.html and is not a new decision — it is that decision made concrete in personas, jobs and a decision-making unit that copy and creative can use directly.

Why downsizers first. They are the largest, calmest and most reachable segment, with no crisis trigger required to convert, which makes them the best fit for the always-on channels (radio, community, referral) this business already has some reach in. They are also the segment where a repeatable, less Adam-dependent journey can be designed and tested without the emotional risk of getting a grieving or time-pressured seller's experience wrong.

Why estate/health-driven sellers second. Smaller in month-to-month volume, but the highest advocacy value of any segment — get the tone right here and it compounds into referral for the other segments, because this is the segment most likely to talk about the experience afterwards, not just the transaction. It is also the segment where the decision-maker is not the car's owner, which changes the messaging and journey design in ways worth building for deliberately rather than leaving implicit.

Licence-loss sellers remain a real, high-intent segment, captured opportunistically through the same always-on channels and a fast, low-friction first-contact experience — but this engagement does not yet build a dedicated acquisition channel for a private, hard-to-predict trigger, consistent with the "watch, don't build yet" call in customer-segments.html pending CQ06 (lead-source mix). The premium/low-kilometre niche (Segment 4 in customer-segments.html) stays out of scope for this document entirely; it has no confirmed volume or margin and is not a target for Design-phase work.

Every call above departs from nothing in Discover — it is Discover's own priority call, operationalised. All of it remains a hypothesis pending Adam's CORE answers, especially CQ06 (lead source) and CQ07/CQ16 (referral mechanism), which would materially change how much weight advocacy-driven design deserves relative to paid or owned reach.

Detailed buyer personas

Persona 1 (primary) — Barbara, 71, the downsizer

Day in the life. Barbara gets up, has tea on the back porch, and the second car — her late husband's, or the one she no longer trusts herself to drive comfortably in Gold Coast traffic — sits in the driveway, registered, insured, and not going anywhere. She has mentioned to her daughter twice this month that she "really should do something about that car," and both times has done nothing, because the thought of listing it, photographing it, and fielding calls from strangers is more effort than the money is worth to her. She half-listens to 4CRB in the kitchen most mornings and has heard Adam's segment before, without ever quite deciding it applied to her. Today, a registration renewal notice arrives in the mail, and the nagging task becomes a slightly less avoidable one.

Decision criteria, ranked.

  1. Trust in the person she is dealing with, above all else
  2. A fair number, explained in plain language she doesn't have to negotiate on
  3. As little effort, technology and stranger-contact as possible
  4. Speed of payment, once she has decided to go ahead
  5. Price maximisation — present, but consistently lower priority than the first three

Information sources. 4CRB radio (background listening, not active research); her adult children, consulted for reassurance rather than decision-making authority; neighbours and community networks; word of mouth from anyone she knows who has sold a car this way before. [Inferred — channel weighting not yet confirmed with Adam, pending CQ06.]

Objection patterns.

  • "Is the valuation actually free, or is there a catch I'm not seeing?"
  • "Will I be obligated to sell once someone has come out and looked at it?"
  • "I don't love the idea of a stranger coming to the house."
  • "My car is older / has done a lot of kilometres — will they even want it?"
  • "I'll get to it eventually" — general inertia, not a hard objection but the most common one to overcome first

Communication preferences. Phone call over form or app, every time; warm and unhurried in tone, never rushed; radio and word of mouth as trust-builders ahead of any direct pitch; printed or plainly worded material over anything that assumes digital fluency. [Inferred from segment description in audience-teardown.html and customer-segments.html; not tested directly with a downsizer customer.]

Persona 2 (secondary, highest advocacy) — Jen, 46, the family seller

Day in the life. Jen is managing her father's affairs after a health decline that means he can no longer drive and has moved, or is about to move, into care. Selling his car is one item on a list that also includes his medications, his mail, his bills and a hundred small emotionally loaded decisions. She is not shopping around — she is looking for the fastest way to get this one thing handled properly without adding to what already feels like an overwhelming week. [Modelled directly on the site's own Currumbin Waters testimonial: "My father is unwell and finds it difficult to leave the house. Dad was so grateful Adam was able to help."]

Decision criteria, ranked.

  1. Being treated with care and respect, not as a transaction
  2. Minimal steps and minimal demands on her time and her father's
  3. Confidence the outcome is fair to her father, since she is acting on his behalf
  4. Someone coming to them, rather than the family organising anything
  5. Speed — present, but secondary to feeling the process was handled properly

Information sources. Referral from a friend or colleague who has been through something similar; word of mouth generally, since this segment is unlikely to be actively searching before the trigger event; general search when researching "how to sell a parent's car," if she reaches for a search engine or an AI assistant at all. [Inferred — not a confirmed channel.]

Objection patterns.

  • "Will this feel transactional, given everything else going on?"
  • "Does my father need to be present, or can I handle this on his behalf?"
  • "Am I getting a fair price for him, given I'm not the one who'd usually make this call?"
  • "Will there be pressure to decide quickly, when I need to move at my own pace?"
  • "Is this going to take multiple visits or a lot of back-and-forth?"

Communication preferences. Phone first, with clear, simple next steps stated up front; patient, unhurried follow-up rather than a sales cadence; written confirmation of what was agreed, useful for her own peace of mind and any family member she needs to update. [Inferred; the emotional register here is the most sensitive of any segment and the most likely to be misjudged if design defaults to a standard sales-follow-up pattern.]

Persona 3 (opportunistic, not a dedicated build target) — Graham, 78, the licence-loss seller

Day in the life. Graham's licence was recently revoked or voluntarily surrendered on medical advice. He is proud, does not want the situation broadcast, and now has a fixed, fairly short window in which the car needs to go. He is not interested in a drawn-out process or in explaining his situation more than once.

Decision criteria, ranked.

  1. Speed — the timeline is externally forced, not chosen
  2. Discretion — not wanting the situation fussed over or made into a bigger event
  3. A fair price, with real worry that urgency will be read as desperation and exploited
  4. Minimal explanation required of his personal circumstances

Information sources. Word of mouth from someone who has been through the same licence-loss situation; general radio awareness ahead of the trigger; a possible referral from a GP, optometrist or driving-assessment service. [Hypothesis — no confirmed referral-partner channel exists yet; untested idea, not a validated channel.]

Objection patterns.

  • "Because I have to sell now, will I be lowballed?"
  • "Will this drag on when I need it done this week?"
  • "Do I have to explain why I'm selling?"
  • "Can I trust a stranger with this, given I don't have time to shop around and compare?"

Communication preferences. Fast, direct, respectful — no small talk that reads as slowing him down; a single phone call that gets a clear answer; no drawn-out written correspondence. Captured opportunistically through the same always-on channels built for downsizers rather than a dedicated funnel — see the targeting call above.

Jobs-to-be-done analysis

Building on jobs-to-be-done.html, this engagement chooses to serve the following functional, emotional and social jobs for the two priority personas.

Barbara (downsizer) — jobs we serve

Functional job. "When I have an extra car I no longer need, I want it sold and gone with minimal effort on my part, so I can be free of the ongoing cost and clutter without a project." Current solution: letting it sit, or a reluctant, unfinished attempt at listing it privately. What's broken: every alternative requires effort, technology or stranger contact she'd rather avoid. Done well, in her words: "One phone call, one visit, and it was gone — I didn't have to do anything."

Emotional job. "When I sell a car I've had for years, I want to feel like I haven't been taken advantage of, so I can feel good about the decision rather than second-guessing it." Current solution: accepting a dealership trade-in number without real confidence it's fair. What's broken: no independent-feeling way to know if a number is right. Done well, in her words: "He explained it properly — I understood why it was worth what it was."

Social job. "When I tell my family or friends I've sold the car, I want to be seen as having handled it sensibly, so I don't feel like I was careless with something that mattered." Current solution: asking an adult child to check the number or handle the listing for her. What's broken: this transfers the burden to someone else rather than resolving it herself. Done well, in her words: "I sorted it myself, and it went exactly the way it should have."

Jen (family seller) — jobs we serve

Functional job. "When I'm managing my father's affairs, I want his car handled properly by someone else, so I can focus on him instead of a transaction." Current solution: doing it herself, reluctantly, alongside everything else, or leaving the car unsold longer than necessary. What's broken: every DIY option adds a task to an already overloaded season, with no one taking it off her plate entirely. Done well, in her words: "One call, one visit, and it was off my list."

Emotional job. "When I sell my father's car on his behalf, I want to feel it was handled with the same care I'd want for him, so I don't carry guilt about how it was done." Current solution: anxious, unresolved handling by family, often delayed. What's broken: no guarantee the process will be gentle rather than transactional. Done well, in her words: "They were kind about it — it didn't feel like just another sale to them."

Social job. "When I handle this for my father, I want to be seen by my family as having done right by him, so I don't feel judged for how the situation was managed." Current solution: managing it quietly and hoping no one questions the outcome. What's broken: without a fair, well-explained number, second-guessing from other family members is a real risk. Done well, in her words: "Everyone agreed it was handled properly — no one had any complaints."

Empathy map — Barbara (primary persona)

Think. "Am I going to get ripped off because I don't really know what this car is worth? Is it even worth the hassle of dealing with someone I don't know?"

Feel. A low, background anxiety about being taken advantage of, mixed with mild guilt that the car has been sitting unused for months and she "should have dealt with it by now."

Say. "I really should do something about that car." "I don't want to deal with strangers coming through." "I heard about this fellow on the radio — I think he's the one who buys cars."

Do. Mentions the car to her adult children without acting; half-listens to the radio without calling in; occasionally looks at what similar cars are listed for online, then closes the tab without doing anything about it.

Hear. Adam's segment on 4CRB's "What's Your Car Worth?"; a neighbour or friend mentioning they sold their car "to that bloke off the radio"; her children gently suggesting she "just get rid of it."

See. The car itself in the driveway, unused; a registration renewal notice; other people's cars for sale on Facebook Marketplace, which she has no interest in replicating herself.

Decision-making unit

This is a B2C decision, with one structural inversion worth naming plainly: the "customer" is the seller, and the business pays them, rather than the more usual pattern of a customer paying the business. That inversion matters for how the decision-making unit is read below.

Downsizers (Barbara). Decision Maker: Barbara herself — she owns the car and makes the call unassisted. Influencer: adult children, consulted informally for reassurance rather than approval; occasionally a friend who has been through the same process. Payer (inverted — the party transferring funds): Adam / the business, once a price is agreed.

Estate/health-driven sellers (Jen). Decision Maker: the adult child or family member acting with delegated authority — she can say yes without her father present for every step. Influencer: other family members with a view on price or timing, and the parent themselves where they are able to weigh in; the risk of family friction here is flagged as a plausible but unconfirmed pain in customer-pain-points.html. Payer (inverted): Adam / the business, once a price is agreed with the family's authorised decision-maker.

Licence-loss sellers (Graham). Decision Maker: Graham himself, though a family member may be present in the background given the circumstances of the licence loss. Influencer: family involved in the original licence situation; occasionally a GP or driving-assessment contact. Payer (inverted): Adam / the business.

See customer-journey.html for how this decision-making unit moves through the stages from first exposure to advocacy, and messaging-by-stage.html for what each party in this unit needs to hear at each point.
